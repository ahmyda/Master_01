1. Introduction

Research methodology provides the backbone of scholarly inquiry in applied linguistics, literature, and education. Understanding the different types of research and the appropriate approaches (quantitative, qualitative, or mixed) is essential for designing valid, ethical, and impactful studies. This lecture synthesizes foundational knowledge from two core lectures: Types of Research and Research Approaches, offering a comprehensive guide for Master’s students beginning their research journey.

“The literature review is not a step—it is an ongoing dialogue with existing knowledge.” — Kumar (2011, p. 32)

2. Types of Research

2.1 Pure (Basic/Fundamental) Research

Aims to generate knowledge for its own sake, driven by curiosity rather than immediate application (Tavakoli, 2013).

Example:

Studying how children acquire syntax without focusing on classroom teaching methods.

2.2 Applied (Action) Research

Addresses real-world problems using empirical methods, with direct practical outcomes.

Example:

Designing and testing a new digital tool to improve vocabulary retention in high school learners.

2.3 Descriptive Research

Provides systematic descriptions of phenomena by answering: who, what, when, where, how.

Common in journals due to its objectivity and clarity.

Example:

Surveying student demographics (age, L1, socioeconomic status) in an ESL program.

2.4 Correlational Research

Examines relationships between two or more variables using statistical analysis—without implying causation (Tavakoli, 2013).

Example:

Exploring the correlation between screen time and writing proficiency among university students.

2.5 Explanatory Research

Seeks to explain why or how a phenomenon occurs, often testing causal relationships.

Example:

Investigating how task-based learning impacts student motivation in online classrooms.

2.6 Exploratory Research

Used when little is known about a topic; helps define problems and generate hypotheses.

Example:

Understanding teachers’ initial attitudes toward AI-assisted grading tools.

3. Quantitative vs. Qualitative Approaches

3.1 Quantitative Research

Focuses on objectivity, measurement, and statistical analysis. Converts phenomena into numerical data to identify patterns and test hypotheses.

Common techniques: surveys, experiments, SPSS analysis, distributional analysis.

Strengths: Generalizable, replicable.
Limitations: May overlook context, emotion, and subjective meaning.

Common Biases in Quantitative Research

  • Sampling Bias: Overrepresenting one group (e.g., only Western literature).
  • Measurement Bias: Leading or poorly worded survey questions.
  • Analysis Bias: Selectively reporting only favorable results.

3.2 Qualitative Research

Seeks deep understanding of human behavior, beliefs, and social contexts through non-numerical data (interviews, observations, case studies).

Common in social sciences and education (Mackey & Gass, 2010).

Strengths: Rich, contextual insights.
Limitations: Subject to researcher bias; not easily generalizable.

Common Biases in Qualitative Research

  • Researcher’s Personal Bias: Interpreting data to match personal beliefs.
  • Interpretative Bias: Misreading participant statements (e.g., assuming “pressure” = negativity).
  • Selection Bias: Interviewing only one subgroup (e.g., students from one department).
“Both approaches are open to bias—what matters is methodological rigor and transparency.” — Tavakoli (2013)

4. Mixed Methods and Triangulation

4.1 Mixed Methods Approach

Combines quantitative and qualitative data in a single study to gain comprehensive insights (Novikov & Novikov, 2013). Numbers provide breadth; narratives provide depth.

Example:

Surveying 200 students about online engagement (quantitative) + interviewing 10 for detailed experiences (qualitative).

4.2 Triangulation

“Measuring the same concept using two or more methods” (Kadushin et al., 2008). Enhances validity and captures multiple dimensions of a phenomenon.

Example:

Studying learner engagement via: (1) observation, (2) self-report surveys, and (3) forum participation logs.

Quick Summary

  • Research types range from pure (theoretical) to applied (practical).
  • Quantitative = numbers, patterns, generalization.
  • Qualitative = meaning, context, depth.
  • Mixed methods + triangulation enhance validity and richness.
  • Literature & Civilization use specialized methods (textual, historical, content analysis).
  • Literature review is a dynamic, foundational process—not a formality.
