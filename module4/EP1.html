Exam Preparation: Sociolinguistics

15 Likely Exam Questions & Model Answers

Q1: What is the first step in the research process, and why is it important?

A: The first step is identifying a research problem. This is crucial because it defines the purpose and direction of the entire study. For example, instead of vaguely studying “education,” a researcher might focus on “the impact of online teaching on student engagement during the COVID-19 pandemic.” A well-defined problem ensures the research is focused, manageable, and contributes new knowledge.

Q2: How do academic research problems differ from everyday problems?

A: Academic research problems aim to create objective, reliable knowledge through systematic inquiry. They often begin as abstract ideas (e.g., “digital inequality”) and evolve into testable questions (e.g., “How does internet access affect homework completion in rural Algerian schools?”). Unlike everyday problems, they must be researchable, grounded in theory, and capable of generating evidence-based answers.

Q3: What are the key purposes of conducting a literature review?

A: A literature review helps (1) identify gaps in existing knowledge, (2) determine if the research is feasible, (3) refine the research question, (4) develop a conceptual framework, and (5) adopt state-of-the-art methods. For instance, reviewing studies on “online learning in North Africa” might reveal that attitudes of university students in Algeria are underexplored—guiding a new study.

Q4: How does a literature review shape the research design?

A: By analyzing prior studies, researchers learn which methods worked (or failed), what variables were measured, and how data was interpreted. For example, if past surveys on teacher stress used a 5-point Likert scale, a new researcher might adopt the same tool for consistency and comparability—enhancing reliability and validity.

Q5: What is a hypothesis, and how does it function in quantitative research?

A: A hypothesis is a testable prediction about the relationship between variables. In quantitative research, it is formulated early and guides the entire design. For example: “Students who attend live online classes will score higher on exams than those who only watch recorded lectures.” This directs data collection (exam scores, attendance type) and statistical testing (t-tests or ANOVA).

Q6: Why might a qualitative study not start with a formal hypothesis?

A: Qualitative research is often exploratory. Instead of testing predictions, it seeks to understand complex phenomena (e.g., “How do Algerian high school teachers experience remote teaching?”). Hypotheses may emerge gradually from data during analysis—allowing flexibility and responsiveness to participants’ perspectives rather than forcing preconceived ideas.

Q7: According to Claire Selltiz (1960), what is research design?

A: Research design is “the arrangement of conditions for collection and analysis of data in a way that combines relevance to the research purpose with economy of procedure.” For example, choosing a case study over a nationwide survey when studying one innovative school saves time and resources while still yielding rich, relevant insights.

Q8: What are three key goals of a strong research design?

A: (1) Limit the scope of research questions to make them answerable; (2) minimize bias through random sampling or blinded analysis; and (3) maximize reliability by using consistent procedures. For instance, using the same interview protocol across all participants ensures fairness and repeatability.

Q9: Why is data collection more than just “gathering information”?

A: As Sapsford & Jupp (2006) note, data collection builds the evidence base for research arguments. For example, recording classroom interactions isn’t just note-taking—it’s capturing authentic language use to analyze teacher-student power dynamics. The quality and relevance of this data directly determine whether conclusions are credible.

Q10: What distinguishes data analysis from data interpretation?

A: Analysis is technical and objective: cleaning data, coding responses, running statistics, or identifying themes. Interpretation is more subjective: explaining what those patterns mean. For example, analysis might show 70% of students felt isolated online; interpretation would link this to lack of peer interaction and suggest policy implications.

Q11: What are common steps in data analysis?

A: Researchers (1) correct errors and remove irrelevant entries, (2) transform raw data into a usable format (e.g., transcribing interviews or coding survey responses), (3) identify patterns (e.g., frequent complaints about internet access), and (4) use visualizations like bar charts or word clouds to clarify findings before drawing conclusions.

Q12: What role does “feedback (F)” play in the research process?

A: Feedback (F) helps control and refine sub-systems during research. For example, if pilot interviews reveal confusing questions, the researcher revises the interview guide before full data collection—ensuring better quality. This cyclical adjustment improves validity and responsiveness throughout the process.

Q13: What is “feed forward (F_F)” and how is it used in research?

A: Feed forward (F_F) provides criteria for future evaluation. For instance, during the design phase, a researcher might set benchmarks: “If less than 50% of survey responses are complete, the data will be discarded.” These pre-established standards guide decisions later, ensuring rigor and consistency.

Q14: What are the essential components of a standard research report?

A: A standard report includes: (1) Title, (2) Abstract, (3) Introduction, (4) Literature Review, (5) Method, (6) Results, (7) Discussion, (8) References, and (9) Appendices. For example, the Method section details how 120 students were surveyed using a validated questionnaire, allowing others to replicate the study—supporting transparency and scientific integrity.

Q15: What characteristics define a strong academic research report?

A: It must be formal (structured and professional), objective (free from personal bias), ethical (respecting participant consent and confidentiality), accurate (based on verified data), and structured (following standard sections). For instance, stating “62% of participants agreed” is accurate; claiming “everyone hated online classes” would be unscientific and biased.
