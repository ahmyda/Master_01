Core Concept: Rationalism

What is Rationalism?

Rationalism is the epistemological view that reason—not sensory experience—is the primary source of knowledge.

  • Knowledge can be a priori (independent of experience)
  • Associated with thinkers like Descartes, Spinoza, and Leibniz
  • Applies to language via innate structures (e.g., Universal Grammar)
Key Components
  • Innate ideas
  • Prior knowledge through reason
  • Deductive reasoning
  • Mind-world correspondence
Key Insight

Rationalism underpins Chomsky’s claim that linguistic knowledge is not learned from input alone but is partially innate, challenging empiricist views of language acquisition.

Explaining Rationalism in SLA

Innate Linguistic Knowledge

Humans are born with Universal Grammar (UG)—a biological endowment that constrains possible grammars.

Language as Maturation

Language acquisition resembles growth; input triggers parameter setting but doesn’t “teach” grammar.

L2 and UG Access

Debate continues: Can adult L2 learners access UG? Some (e.g., Flynn & Martohardjono, 1994) argue yes; others suggest reliance on general cognition.

Key Rationalist Concepts

Innate Ideas

Certain knowledge is hardwired into the human mind prior to experience.

Deductive Reasoning

Truths are derived logically from self-evident principles (e.g., “I think, therefore I am”).

Mind–World Correspondence

The rational structure of the mind reflects the rational order of reality.

Language Acquisition in Rationalism

Initial State

Learner begins with Universal Grammar: innate principles and unset parameters.

Input Triggers

Linguistic input sets parameters; it does not provide data to be “learned” inductively.

Parameter Setting

Child’s language faculty fixes parameter values based on primary linguistic data.

Steady State

Mature grammatical competence emerges as the internalized target language grammar.

Implications for SLA

Access to UG in L2?

  • Full access? (Strong rationalist view)
  • Partial or no access? (Maturational constraints)
  • L1 parameter settings may block L2 resetting

Critiques of Rationalism

Poverty of the Stimulus Challenged

Pullum & Scholz (2002): Input may be richer than claimed; positive evidence is more abundant.

Usage-Based Alternatives

Tomasello (2003): Language emerges from item-based learning and generalization, not innate triggers.

Cross-Linguistic Variation

Greater typological diversity than UG predicts—challenges notion of strict universal constraints.

Individual Differences

Variation in acquisition paths contradicts uniformity expected under biological maturation model.

Critical Thinking Questions
  • Does the Critical Period Hypothesis support or challenge UG-based SLA theories?
  • Can rationalism account for the role of social interaction in language learning?
  • How might empiricist critiques reshape our understanding of L2 input?
  • Is parameter resetting possible in adult L2 learners? Why or why not?
