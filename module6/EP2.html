Exam Preparation: Language Acquisition

15 Likely Exam Questions & Model Answers

Q1: What is “acquisition” in SLA?

A: Acquisition is the natural, unconscious internalization of a language system through meaningful interaction and exposure—not through explicit instruction. It is distinct from conscious “learning” and mirrors how children acquire their first language.

Q2: What are the four key components of language acquisition?

A: (1) Phonological processing (sound systems), (2) Morpho-syntactic development (grammar), (3) Semantic networks (meaning), and (4) Pragmatic competence (contextual and social use of language).

Q3: What is Krashen’s (1985) key insight about acquisition?

A: Krashen emphasized that linguistic development occurs implicitly through meaningful interaction—not through grammar drills or explicit teaching. Comprehensible input in engaging contexts drives acquisition.

Q4: How do nativist approaches explain language acquisition?

A: They argue that humans possess innate linguistic capacities (e.g., Chomsky’s Universal Grammar) that guide acquisition. Children are biologically prepared to acquire language rapidly and uniformly, even from limited input.

Q5: What role does social interaction play in language development?

A: According to social-interactionist theories (Bruner, Vygotsky), communicative context, scaffolding, and meaningful dialogue drive acquisition. Language develops through negotiation of meaning and culturally mediated input.

Q6: What is the current consensus on how language is acquired?

A: A combination of innate predispositions, statistical learning (detecting patterns in input), and social motivation—integrating insights from nativist, cognitive, and interactionist perspectives.

Q7: How does behaviorist theory explain language learning?

A: Skinner (1957) viewed language as habit formation through imitation, reinforcement, and operant conditioning—e.g., a child repeats “milk” after being rewarded, gradually shaping verbal behavior.

Q8: What is Chomsky’s Language Acquisition Device (LAD)?

A: It is an innate mental faculty containing Universal Grammar—principles common to all human languages—that allows children to deduce grammatical rules from minimal input, explaining the creativity and speed of acquisition.

Q9: What happens in the pre-linguistic stage (0–12 months)?

A: Infants progress from crying to cooing to babbling, develop categorical perception of speech sounds, and become attuned to the phonology of their native language(s).

Q10: Describe the one-word (holophrastic) stage.

A: Occurring at 12–18 months, children use single words to express complete thoughts (e.g., “milk” = “I want milk”). Comprehension far exceeds production, and vocabulary reaches about 50 words.

Q11: What characterizes the two-word stage (18–24 months)?

A: Children produce telegraphic speech (e.g., “Mommy go”), showing emergent syntax and rapid vocabulary growth—while omitting function words like articles or auxiliaries.

Q12: What is overgeneralization, and when does it occur?

A: It’s the active application of grammatical rules to irregular forms (e.g., “goed” instead of “went”), typically in the multi-word stage (24–30 months), showing rule-governed, not imitative, learning.

Q13: How does language develop during the school-age years (6–12)?

A: Children develop metalinguistic awareness (thinking about language), literacy enhances grammatical knowledge, and they acquire academic language needed for formal education.

Q14: Why does acquisition vs. learning matter for teaching?

A: If acquisition occurs through meaningful use, teaching should prioritize communication, comprehensible input, and interaction—rather than isolated grammar drills or error correction.

Q15: Which theory best explains children’s creative language use?

A: The nativist theory—because children produce novel, grammatical sentences they’ve never heard (e.g., “I goed to park”), suggesting internal rule generation, not imitation or habit formation.
