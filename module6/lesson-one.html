1. Key Terms in SLA

"Understanding Second Language Acquisition requires a precise command of its specialized terminology."

SLA, often considered a sub-discipline of applied linguistics, investigates how individuals learn a language subsequent to their native (first) language. Central to this study are several theoretical constructs that define the learning process and its outcomes.

Mother Tongue L1 vs Second/Foreign Tongue L2

Mother Tongue (L1)

The first language acquired naturally in infancy, providing the cognitive and linguistic baseline.

Second Language (L2)

Learned in a community where it is widely spoken (e.g., an immigrant learning English in the US).

Foreign Language

Learned in a context where it is not the primary language of communication (e.g., an American student learning Japanese in the US).

Input vs Output

Concept Definition Key Characteristics
Input The language raw material or data a learner hears or reads Comprehensible, abundant, relevant (Krashen's i+1)
Output The language a learner produces (speaking or writing) Noticing function, hypothesis testing, feedback reception (Swain)

Core SLA Concepts

Interlanguage

"Interlanguage refers to the unique linguistic system a second language learner develops, which is structurally intermediate between the learner's L1 and the target L2."
- Larry Selinker (1972)

Interlanguage is not merely a collection of errors; it is systematic and rule-governed, evolving as the learner tests hypotheses about the target language.

Systematicity
Even errors are predictable and based on the learner's current internalized rules.
Permeability
The system is receptive to new L2 input, allowing for change and growth.
Variability
The learner's language use changes depending on context (e.g., more accurate in formal testing, less accurate in spontaneous conversation).

Transfer

Types of Transfer:
  • Positive Transfer: Occurs when L1 structure aligns with L2 structure, accelerating acquisition (e.g., cognates)
  • Negative Transfer (Interference): Occurs when L1 structure conflicts with L2, leading to errors (e.g., word order, phonology)

Fossilization

Definition
The cessation of interlanguage development before the learner reaches target language norms, despite continuous exposure to the L2 or further instruction.
Causes
Attributed to internal mechanisms, potentially related to neural commitment or socio-psychological factors.
Impact
Explains plateaus in learner progress, particularly in phonology and morphology.

Error vs Mistake

Type Definition Characteristics
Error (Competence) A systematic deviation made by a learner who has not yet mastered the target language rule Reveals gaps in underlying knowledge; resistant to self-correction
Mistake (Performance) A random, non-systematic deviation due to factors like fatigue or inattention Reflects lapse in performance; speaker can usually self-correct

2. Perspectives on Language

Defining "language" is a complex task, as it is simultaneously a cognitive mechanism, a behavioral system, and a social construct. SLA theory must account for all three dimensions to fully capture the learning process.

Language as Human Capacity

Rooted in Universal Grammar (Chomsky), views language as an innate, specialized cognitive faculty unique to humans. Governs the acquisition device with biological predisposition.

Language as Human Behavior

Influenced by behaviorist traditions (Skinner), views language as observable verbal behaviors learned through imitation, practice, and reinforcement.

Language as Social Phenomenon

Emphasized by socio-cultural theory (Vygotsky), views language as a tool for social interaction and cultural mediation through negotiation of meaning.

Competence vs Performance

Linguistic Competence
The underlying, often unconscious, knowledge of a language system—the rules of grammar, syntax, and morphology that resides in the mind of the native or fluent speaker.
Linguistic Performance
The actual use of language in concrete situations, subject to nonlinguistic factors like fatigue, memory constraints, and distraction.
Communicative Competence
Dell Hymes (1972) expands on Chomsky, arguing that knowing a language involves not just grammatical rules, but also knowing when, where, and how to use language appropriately in social contexts.

3. Acquisition vs Learning

"Acquisition is the way people develop ability in a first language... Learning is the process that results in knowing about language."
- Stephen Krashen
Aspect Acquisition Learning
Process Subconscious, similar to L1 acquisition Conscious, from formal instruction
Focus Meaning of communication Form of the language
Result Spontaneous, fluent language use Knowledge about language (Monitor)
Application Primary communication system Editor for acquired language output
Monitor Model Conditions:

Learned knowledge (Monitor) can only be used when conditions are met: sufficient time, focus on form, and knowledge of the rule.
