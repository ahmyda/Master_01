Fundamental Concepts

What is Language?

A complex system of communication involving:

  • Phonology (sound systems)
  • Morphology (word formation)
  • Syntax (sentence structure)
  • Semantics (meaning)
  • Pragmatics (contextual use)
What Do We Mean by "Second"?

In SLA context, "second" language refers to:

  • Any language learned after the first language (L1)
  • Includes third, fourth, etc. languages
  • Can be learned in naturalistic or instructional settings
  • Different from foreign language learning contexts

SLA Theory Key Terms

Phenomena Language

The observable aspects of language use and acquisition that theories attempt to explain, including errors, developmental patterns, and variability in learner language.

Acquisition

The process of developing ability in a language through natural exposure and use, often contrasted with conscious "learning" in formal settings.

Explanation

Theoretical accounts that seek to understand why and how second language acquisition occurs, identifying causal factors and mechanisms.

Rationale and Critiques

The underlying logic and assumptions of theories, along with critical evaluations of their limitations and empirical support.

Construction and Assessment

How SLA theories are built, tested, and evaluated through empirical research and theoretical argumentation.

The Role of Universal Grammar

Universal Grammar (UG) in SLA

Noam Chomsky's theory that humans are born with an innate language faculty containing abstract principles common to all human languages. In SLA, UG examines whether and how this innate knowledge influences second language acquisition.

Key UG Positions in SLA

  • Direct Access: L2 learners have full access to UG principles
  • No Access: L2 learners cannot access UG after critical period
  • Indirect Access: UG operates through the L1 knowledge
  • Partial Access: Some UG principles remain accessible

Approaches to SLA

Linguistic Approaches

Focus on language structure and UG

Psychological Approaches

Examine cognitive processes and learning mechanisms

Sociocultural Approaches

Emphasize social interaction and cultural context

Functional Approaches

Focus on language use and communication needs
