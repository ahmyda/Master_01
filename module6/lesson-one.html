Second Language Acquisition - Lesson 1

Foundations of SLA Theory

Fundamental Concepts

What is Language?
  • Phonology (sound systems)
  • Morphology (word formation)
  • Syntax (sentence structure)
  • Semantics (meaning)
  • Pragmatics (contextual use)
What Do We Mean by "Second"?
  • Any language learned after L1
  • Includes third, fourth languages, etc.
  • Learned in naturalistic or instructional settings
  • Different from foreign language learning contexts

SLA Theory Key Terms

Phenomena Language

Observable aspects of language use and acquisition, including errors and patterns.

Acquisition

Developing language ability through natural exposure and use.

Explanation

Theoretical accounts explaining why and how SLA occurs.

Rationale and Critiques

Underlying logic and critical evaluations of theories.

Construction and Assessment

How SLA theories are built, tested, and evaluated.

The Role of Universal Grammar

UG in SLA

Noam Chomsky's idea: humans have innate language knowledge. SLA explores its effect on L2 learning.

Key UG Positions

  • Direct Access: Full access to UG principles
  • No Access: UG inaccessible after critical period
  • Indirect Access: UG operates via L1 knowledge
  • Partial Access: Some UG principles remain accessible

Approaches to SLA

Linguistic Approaches

Focus on language structure and UG.

Psychological Approaches

Examine cognitive processes and learning mechanisms.

Sociocultural Approaches

Emphasize social interaction and cultural context.

Functional Approaches

Focus on language use and communication needs.

  • What are the main criticisms of the Critical Period Hypothesis?
  • How do sociocultural factors influence SLA outcomes?
  • Compare two major SLA theories and discuss empirical support for each.
