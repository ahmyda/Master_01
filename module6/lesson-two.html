Core Concept: Acquisition

What is Acquisition?

Natural, unconscious internalization of a language system, distinct from conscious "learning".

  • Occurs through meaningful interaction and exposure
  • Not through explicit instruction
  • Example: Children acquiring their first language
Key Components
  • Phonological Processing (sounds)
  • Morpho-syntactic Development (grammar)
  • Semantic Networks (meaning)
  • Pragmatic Competence (contextual use)
Key Insight

Krashen (1985) emphasized the implicit nature of linguistic development through meaningful interaction rather than explicit instruction.

Explaining Language Acquisition

Nativist Approaches

Innate linguistic capacities guide acquisition (Chomsky, 1965).

Social-Interactionist Theories

Communicative context and input drive language development (Bruner, 1983; Vygotsky, 1978).

Current Research Consensus

Combination of innate predispositions, statistical learning, and social motivation.

Theories of Language Acquisition

Behaviorist Theory

Key Figure: B.F. Skinner (1957)

Habit formation through operant conditioning, imitation, and reinforcement.

Nativist Theory

Key Figure: Noam Chomsky (1965)

Innate linguistic knowledge (LAD) with Universal Grammar principles.

Interactionist Theory

Key Figures: Bruner, Vygotsky

Social context, scaffolding, and communicative functions shape development.

Stages of Language Acquisition

Pre-linguistic Stage

0-12 months

  • Crying → cooing → babbling
  • Development of categorical perception
  • Attunement to native phonology

One-Word Stage

12-18 months

  • Holophrastic speech
  • Comprehension exceeds production
  • ~50 words vocabulary

Two-Word Stage

18-24 months

  • Telegraphic speech
  • Emergent syntax
  • Rapid vocabulary expansion

Multi-Word Stage

24-30 months

  • Expanded sentence structures
  • Overgeneralization of rules
  • Active, rule-governed learning

Later Stages

Preschool (3-5 years)

  • Complex sentences with embedded clauses
  • Mastery of grammatical morphemes
  • Improved narrative and pragmatic skills

School-Age (6-12 years)

  • Metalinguistic awareness
  • Literacy acquisition aids grammar
  • Academic language development

Adolescence (13+ years)

  • Advanced pragmatic competence
  • Abstract language understanding
  • Control over stylistic variation
