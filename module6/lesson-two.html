Core Concept: Acquisition

What is Acquisition?

"Acquisition" refers to the natural, unconscious internalization of a language system, distinct from conscious "learning."

  • Occurs through meaningful interaction and exposure
  • Not through explicit instruction
  • Example: Children acquiring their first language
Key Components

Acquisition involves integrating multiple cognitive systems:

  • Phonological Processing (sounds)
  • Morpho-syntactic Development (grammar)
  • Semantic Networks (meaning)
  • Pragmatic Competence (contextual use)
Key Insight

Krashen (1985) popularized the distinction between acquisition and learning, emphasizing the implicit nature of linguistic development through meaningful interaction rather than explicit instruction.

Explaining Language Acquisition

Nativist Approaches

Emphasize innate linguistic capacities that guide acquisition (Chomsky, 1965). Focus on Universal Grammar and biological predispositions.

Social-Interactionist Theories

Stress the significant role of communicative contexts and input in language development (Bruner, 1983; Vygotsky, 1978).

Current Research Consensus

Acquisition likely involves a combination of innate predispositions, statistical learning, and social motivation driving communicative development.

Theories of Language Acquisition

Behaviorist Theory

Key Figure: B.F. Skinner (1957)

Language acquisition as habit formation through operant conditioning, imitation, and reinforcement.

Nativist Theory

Key Figure: Noam Chomsky (1965)

Humans possess innate linguistic knowledge (LAD) with Universal Grammar principles and parameters.

Interactionist Theory

Key Figures: Bruner (1983), Vygotsky (1978)

Emphasizes social context, scaffolding, and communicative functions in language development.

Stages of Language Acquisition

Pre-linguistic Stage

0-12 months

  • Crying → cooing (2 months) → canonical babbling (6-10 months)
  • Development of categorical perception
  • Attunement to native language phonology

One-Word Stage

12-18 months

  • First true words with holophrastic speech
  • Comprehension exceeds production
  • Vocabulary reaches ~50 words by 18 months

Two-Word Stage

18-24 months

  • Telegraphic speech with two-word combinations
  • Emergent understanding of syntax
  • Rapid vocabulary expansion

Multi-Word Stage

24-30 months

  • Sentence structures expand with function words and inflections
  • Overgeneralization of grammatical rules
  • Indicates active, rule-governed learning

Later Stages of Language Acquisition

Preschool Period (3-5 years)

  • Complex sentences with embedded clauses and passive voice
  • Mastery of basic grammatical morphemes
  • Improved narrative abilities and pragmatic skills

School-Age Period (6-12 years)

  • Development of metalinguistic awareness
  • Literacy acquisition facilitates grammatical understanding
  • Acquisition of academic language registers

Adolescence (13+ years)

  • Advanced pragmatic competence (irony, sarcasm)
  • Deeper comprehension of abstract language
  • Greater control over stylistic variation
Critical Thinking Questions

  • How do the stages of language acquisition inform our understanding of second language learning?
