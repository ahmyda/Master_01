Interculturality - Introduction

1. Introduction to Interculturality

Interculturality is the ability to understand, respect, and interact effectively with people from different cultural backgrounds. In a globalized world, it helps promote peaceful coexistence, tolerance, and collaboration.

"Interculturality is not only about knowing other cultures, but also learning to live and work together with them." — UNESCO (2013)

2. What is Culture?

Culture refers to the shared beliefs, traditions, and practices that shape the behavior and identity of a community. It influences how people think, feel, and act in their daily lives.

  • Beliefs: Ideas that people accept as true (e.g., religion, family values).
  • Traditions: Customs passed through generations (e.g., holidays, clothing, food).
  • Values: Standards of what is considered right or wrong in a society.
Examples:
  • In Japan, bowing is a sign of respect, while in Western countries, a handshake is more common.
  • In some Arab cultures, hospitality is highly valued, and guests are treated with great honor.

According to Edward T. Hall (1976), culture is “the silent language” that shapes how people interpret the world.

3. What is Communication?

Communication is the exchange of information, ideas, and feelings between individuals or groups. It is both verbal and non-verbal, and it always reflects the culture of the speakers.

  • Language choice, tone, and politeness vary across cultures.
  • Gestures, eye contact, and silence carry different meanings depending on the context.
Examples:
  • In the U.S., direct communication is appreciated, while in many Asian cultures, indirect communication is preferred to avoid conflict.
  • Maintaining eye contact is seen as confidence in some countries, but as disrespect in others.

As Hall (1959) stated, “communication is culture and culture is communication.”

4. What is Interculturality?

Interculturality goes beyond simply comparing cultures — it emphasizes interaction, mutual learning, and respect among people from different backgrounds. It is an ongoing process of understanding and adaptation.

  • Encourages dialogue and empathy between individuals and groups.
  • Promotes equality and appreciation of cultural diversity.
  • Helps overcome prejudice, stereotypes, and discrimination.
Examples:
  • Students from different countries studying together learn new ways of thinking and behaving.
  • In multinational companies, intercultural training improves teamwork and communication.

Bennett (1998) describes intercultural competence as the ability to shift perspective and adapt behavior in culturally appropriate ways.

Quick Summary

Interculturality connects culture and communication. Understanding cultural differences allows people to communicate effectively, reduce misunderstandings, and build respectful relationships.

  • Culture: Shared beliefs and traditions shaping identity.
  • Communication: Reflects and expresses cultural values.
  • Interculturality: Learning, adapting, and collaborating across cultures.

Example: A teacher who understands students’ cultural backgrounds can make lessons more inclusive and effective.
