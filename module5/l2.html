1. What is Grammar?

Grammar is the study of form and structure in language—the system that governs how words combine to create meaningful communication.

Basic Definition
Grammar is the business of taking a language to pieces, to see how it works. (David Crystal)
Dynamic Perspective
Grammar is not a set of fixed rules created in advance, but a system that emerges as people use language and develop patterns of communication. It reflects how a language is used at a particular point in time and naturally changes as the language evolves.
Functional Role
Grammar adds meanings that are not easily inferable from the immediate context.
Example

Acceptable: "We are not at home."
Unacceptable: "We not are at home."

Three Aspects of Grammar:

Aspect Description Example
1. Form The grammatical structure or pattern Passive voice structure: "The car was stuck."
2. Meaning The semantic value or interpretation Focus on the agent (who/what performed the action)
3. Use The communicative purpose or function To show that the doer is not important or unknown

2. Challenges of Teaching Grammar

Teaching grammar presents several significant challenges for both teachers and learners in second language acquisition.

a) The Abstract Nature of Rules
Grammar rules can be abstract and difficult for learners to grasp conceptually.
Example

"I had finished my homework." vs
"I had finished my homework before my friend came."
Learners may struggle to understand why the past perfect is used differently in these contexts.

b) Contextual Variation
The same grammatical structure can have different meanings or uses in different contexts.
Example

"I don't know anything." vs "I know nothing."
These have the same meaning but use different grammatical structures.

c) Engagement Issues
Grammar instruction can often feel dry, disconnected from real communication, and difficult to make engaging for learners.
d) Language Transfer
Learners' first language (L1) grammar can interfere with their acquisition of second language (L2) grammar, leading to errors and confusion.

3. Theoretical Approaches to Grammar

Different linguistic theories offer varying perspectives on what grammar is and how it should be studied and taught.

1. Prescriptive vs. Descriptive Grammar

Approach Focus Perspective
Prescriptive Grammar Rules for "correct" language use Normative - how language should be used
Descriptive Grammar Language as it is naturally used Analytical - how language is used

2. Generative Grammar (Noam Chomsky)

Core Concept
A theory that posits that the human mind has an innate ability to generate an infinite number of sentences, including ones never heard before, by following a set of rules or principles.
Key Features
These rules allow for the creation of grammatical sentences and the identification of ungrammatical ones, suggesting a universal grammar common to all humans.

3. Functional Grammar (Michael Halliday)

Core Concept
A theory concerned with how language functions in context and how people use language to make meaning in different social situations.
Key Features
Unlike traditional structuralist approaches that prioritize formal aspects like syntax, functional grammar emphasizes the social and communicative purposes of language.

4. Cognitive Grammar (Ronald Langacker)

Core Concept
A linguistic theory that views language as grounded in human cognition, with grammar closely tied to meaning and cognitive abilities.
Key Features
Grammar is seen as a reflection of how we conceptualize the world, shaped by mental processes like perception, attention, and memory.
"According to Cognitive Grammar, language is not separate from meaning but is closely tied to our cognitive abilities and experiences."
- Ronald Langacker

4. The Grammar Debate: For vs Against

The role of explicit grammar instruction in second language acquisition has been debated for decades, with strong arguments on both sides.

Arguments FOR Teaching Grammar

Clear Framework for Production
Provides learners with a clear framework for producing accurate and meaningful sentences, helping them grasp complex concepts like verb tenses, word order, and agreement.
Prevents Fossilization
Helps reduce the risk of fossilization of incorrect forms, supporting progression from simple, error-prone language to more complex and accurate use.
Promotes Learner Autonomy
Equips learners with tools to monitor and correct their own errors, promoting greater autonomy and confidence in the learning process.
Supports Pattern Recognition
Encourages learners to notice and analyze patterns in the target language, supporting deeper comprehension.
Manages L1 Transfer
Helps learners manage L1 transfer and avoid errors caused by negative first-language interference.
"Integrating explicit grammar teaching with communicative activities offers a balanced approach that fosters both accuracy and fluency in L2 acquisition."
- Pro-Grammar Teaching Perspective

Arguments AGAINST Teaching Grammar

Hinders Fluency Development
Excessive focus on grammar can hinder fluency development, causing learners to prioritize correctness over meaning, leading to anxiety and fear of mistakes.
Focus on Memorization Over Use
When isolated from meaningful use, learners may focus on memorizing rules rather than engaging in real-life language practice.
Natural Acquisition Through Exposure
Language acquisition often occurs most effectively through exposure and immersion (Krashen's Input Hypothesis), with grammar emerging implicitly through comprehensible input.
Increases Anxiety
Explicit grammar teaching may increase anxiety and detract from communicative and cultural aspects of language use.
Communication-Focused Alternatives
Learners can develop grammatical competence through sustained, meaningful communication without primary focus on abstract rules.
Balanced Perspective

Most contemporary approaches advocate for a balanced integration: explicit grammar instruction when beneficial, combined with ample opportunities for meaningful communication practice.

5. Historical Changes in Grammar Teaching

Approaches to grammar teaching have evolved significantly over time, reflecting changing theories of language learning.

Traditional Methods (FOR Grammar Focus)

Grammar-Translation Method (GTM)
Grammar was the center of language pedagogy, with focus on translation, memorization of rules, and literary texts.
Audio-Lingual Method (ALM)
Emphasized pattern drills and habit formation through repetition and reinforcement.
Limitations of Traditional Approaches

• Does not guarantee language learners' preferences or motivation
• Does not provide enough oral and aural practice for communicative competence
• Often leads to knowledge about the language rather than ability to use it

Communicative Approach (AGAINST Grammar Focus)

Communicative Competence (Dell Hymes)
Shifted focus from grammatical accuracy to the ability to use language appropriately in different social contexts.
Knowledge of Language Use
Emphasized what learners can do with language rather than what they know about language.
Krashen's Acquisition-Learning Hypothesis
Distinguished between subconscious acquisition (like L1 learning) and conscious learning (explicit rule knowledge).
"According to Krashen's theory, language is acquired unconsciously through meaningful interaction rather than learned explicitly through rule instruction."
- Stephen Krashen

Contemporary Integrative Approaches

Approach Grammar Focus Key Principle
Focus on Form Selective, contextualized Brief attention to form during communicative activities
Task-Based Learning Emergent, as needed Grammar emerges from task completion and problem-solving
Content-Based Instruction Integrated with content Grammar learned through subject matter instruction
Eclectic Approach Balanced, flexible Combines elements from various methods based on context

Key Takeaways

Understanding the principles and debates surrounding grammar teaching is essential for effective language instruction.

Grammar is Multidimensional
Encompasses form, meaning, and use—all three aspects must be addressed in teaching.
Teaching Presents Challenges
Abstractness, contextual variation, engagement issues, and L1 transfer require careful pedagogical consideration.
Multiple Theoretical Perspectives
Different linguistic theories offer valuable insights into how grammar functions and should be approached.
Ongoing Pedagogical Debate
The role of explicit grammar instruction continues to be debated, with valid arguments on both sides.
Historical Evolution
Approaches have shifted from grammar-centered methods to communicative and integrative approaches.
Balanced Integration
Contemporary best practices often involve integrating explicit grammar instruction with meaningful communicative practice.
Pedagogical Implication

Effective grammar teaching should be:
1. Contextualized - taught in meaningful contexts
2. Balanced - combining explicit instruction with communicative practice
3. Learner-centered - addressing learners' specific needs and challenges
4. Purposeful - clearly linked to communicative goals
