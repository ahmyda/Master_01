1. The Nature of Teaching

"Teaching is not only about transferring knowledge; it's about creating a personalized and meaningful learning experience."

Effective teaching connects form, accuracy, and meaning to help students communicate successfully.

Key Teaching Elements

Personalization

Lessons should relate to students' lives, interests, and needs.

Meaningfulness

Learning is more effective when students find it relevant and purposeful.

Form + Accuracy (ALM Drills)

The Audio-Lingual Method emphasizes repetition and structure to develop correct language use.

Meaning + Communication

Learners need opportunities to use language meaningfully through real-life tasks and activities that promote fluency.

2. The Qualities of an Effective Teacher

"Effective teaching depends not only on knowledge but also on how lessons are delivered and assessed."
- Ryholds (2005)

Main Characteristics

Student Engagement
Keeps learners active, motivated, and involved.
Lesson Delivery
Presents ideas clearly and uses varied techniques (visuals, discussions, etc.).
Assessment
Monitors progress through feedback, quizzes, and reflection.
Optimism and Positivity
Creates a comfortable and encouraging atmosphere.
Time Management
Uses class time wisely to balance activities.
Lesson Structure
Organizes lessons logically (introduction, practice, feedback).

Question Variety

Examples:
  • Reflective Questions: Encourage critical thinking (e.g., "Why do we use this tense here?")
  • Display Questions: Check knowledge (e.g., "What's the past form of go?")

3. Teaching and Context

Teaching is a context-specific activity. An effective teacher considers the students' background and individual differences when planning lessons.

Contextual Factors

Factor Impact on Teaching
Age Younger learners need active, playful methods; adults prefer goal-oriented tasks.
Gender May influence participation style or learning preferences.
Social & Economic Status Affects access to materials, technology, and exposure to language.
Personality & Self-Esteem Confident learners take more risks; shy ones may need more support.
"Teaching is complex and multidimensional, involving emotional, social, and cognitive aspects."

4. Self-Monitoring (SM) in Language Teaching

Self-Monitoring means observing and evaluating your own teaching to understand what works best. It's a reflective process that helps teachers become more aware and effective.

Why Use Self-Monitoring?

  • Promotes critical evaluation of teaching methods
  • Helps identify strengths and areas for improvement
  • Enhances self-awareness and professional growth
  • Leads to better student outcomes through improved instruction

Methods of Self-Monitoring

Video/Audio Recording

Recording lessons for later analysis and reflection.

Peer Observation

Collaborating with colleagues for feedback and team teaching.

Reflective Journals

Writing reflections after each class to track progress and insights.

Student Feedback

Using forms or discussions to gather student perspectives.

5. Self-Monitoring and CPD

CPD (Continuous Professional Development) refers to an ongoing process where teachers continuously develop their skills, knowledge, and professional attitudes. It's a proactive way to remain effective and competitive in the teaching field.

Key Assumptions

Intentional Reflection
Teaching improvement requires intentional reflection, not just experience.
Experience Alone Isn't Enough
Experience alone doesn't guarantee good teaching.
Critical Reflection
Critical reflection helps teachers understand their classroom actions.
Willingness to Evolve
Effective teaching depends on a strong knowledge base and willingness to evolve.

6. The Four Selves in Self-Monitoring

These are aspects of a teacher's self-awareness, based on the Johari Window model:

Type of Self Description Example
Open Self What both the teacher and students know Teacher knows they use humor in class, and students see it too.
Secret Self What only the teacher knows Teacher hides nervousness but students can't tell.
Blind Self What others see but the teacher doesn't Students feel lessons are rushed, but the teacher is unaware.
Hidden Self What is unknown to both teacher and students Potential strengths or weaknesses yet to be discovered.
Insight:

Understanding these four selves helps teachers analyze their behavior and improve classroom performance through greater self-awareness.

7. Reflection and Professional Growth

Developing critical reflection involves taking a step back from daily teaching to observe, analyze, and question one's actions.

Reflective Questions

Understanding

Did my students understand the activity?

Clarity

Were the instructions clear?

Timing

Did I give enough time for practice?

Engagement

How could I make the next lesson more engaging?

"By consistently asking such questions, teachers build awareness and evolve professionally."
