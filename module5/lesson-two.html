Introduction

All teachers want to know what kind of teachers they are, but they need to step back to reflect on their lessons through regular observation of their own teaching. This is a crucial component of ongoing professional development.

Assumptions About Teacher Development

Knowledge Base

Effective teaching relies on a strong knowledge base. Continuous learning and updating pedagogical knowledge is essential for teaching excellence.

Awareness Gap

Teachers are often unaware of their own teaching practices. Without systematic observation, teachers may not recognize patterns in their teaching behavior.

Beyond Experience

Experience alone does not guarantee development. Mere repetition of teaching without reflection does not lead to improvement.

Reflection Power

Critical reflection fosters deeper insight and change. Teachers are in the best position to evaluate and improve their own teaching through reflection and self-assessment.

What is Self-Monitoring?

Definition

"A systematic approach to the observation, evaluation and management of one's own behavior to achieve better understanding and control of one's behavior." - Armstrong and Firth (1984)

Self-monitoring is a method of reflective teaching, which means thinking carefully about how you teach in order to improve. It serves as an approach to assessment that complements other forms of assessment.

The Self-Monitoring Process

To improve as a teacher through self-monitoring, you need to:

  1. Understand your own teaching - identify what you do well and what might need improvement
  2. Collect real information - gather objective data about what happens in your classroom
  3. Use systematic methods - ensure information is collected in an objective, structured way without personal bias
  4. Analyze the information - make informed decisions about what to keep doing and what to change

Why Use Self-Monitoring?

Critical Thinking Prompt: "Critically evaluate the role of self-monitoring (or reflective teaching) in teacher professional development." Discuss how self-monitoring addresses the limitations of initial teacher training, its contribution to reflective practice, and its effectiveness in aligning perceived teaching performance with actual classroom realities.

Beyond Initial Training

Teachers spend relatively short time in training programs, which should mark only the beginning of their professional development journey.

Critical Reflection

Self-monitoring provides opportunities for teachers to reflect critically on their teaching practices and identify areas for improvement.

Reality Alignment

Self-monitoring helps teachers narrow the gap between their perception of their teaching and the actual classroom reality.

What Can Be Learned From Self-Monitoring?

Self-Awareness Dimensions

Self-assessment or self-monitoring helps teachers examine different types of information about their teaching through various awareness dimensions:

The Open Self

Information about teaching behavior that is known to both the teacher and others

The Secret Self

Known to the teacher but not to others - private reflections and intentions

The Blind Self

Known to others but not to the teacher - unconscious teaching habits

The Hidden Self

Unknown to both the teacher and others - untapped potential and possibilities

Through self-monitoring, teachers seek to find out the critical factors that could explain teaching challenges and identify areas for growth.

How is Self-Monitoring Carried Out?

Personal Reflection

Systematic thinking about teaching experiences, lesson outcomes, and student responses. This can include journaling, mental review of lessons, or structured reflection frameworks.

Self-Reporting

Using checklists, teaching logs, or structured forms to document teaching behaviors, strategies used, and student engagement patterns.

Audio/Video Recording

Recording lessons to objectively review teaching practices, classroom interactions, and student responses without relying on memory alone.

Action Research

Systematic investigation of teaching practices through cycles of planning, acting, observing, and reflecting to improve specific aspects of teaching.

Conclusion

Self-monitoring has many applications in language teaching and is most effective when practiced regularly. Self-monitoring techniques give teachers far greater insight into their own teaching than traditional forms of teacher assessment and are simultaneously a simple but effective way of improving the management and understanding of their own teaching.
