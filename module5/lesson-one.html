Introduction: Seeking the Right Method

Research has traditionally focused on improving language teaching through enhancing the quality of methods. However, both researchers and teachers recognize that while a method can be effective for some time, it can never be the perfect way to teach language.

Definition: A teaching method is a set of specifications for how teaching should be accomplished, derived from a particular theory on the nature of language and second language learning.

Historical Teaching Methods

Grammar-Translation Method (GTM)

Uses native language for instruction, focuses on translating texts and memorizing grammatical rules.

Direct Method

Learns target language through speaking and listening. Native language is allowed but limited.

Audio-Lingual Method (ALM)

Emphasizes repetition and drills to create good speaking habits through pattern practice.

The Silent Way

Students discover language rules with minimal teacher talk, using visual aids and problem-solving.

Suggestopedia

Creates a relaxed setting with music and drama to reduce stress and facilitate natural language absorption.

Community Language Learning

Students work together in supportive groups, with teacher as counselor helping expression in target language.

Total Physical Response (TPR)

Links spoken commands with physical actions, developing listening skills before speaking.

Limitations of Teaching Methods

Why Methods Proved Weak and Limited
  • Teaching is complex - involves many factors beyond just methods
  • Methods tend to be rigid - lack flexibility for diverse classroom situations
  • May not suit diverse student needs - different contexts require different approaches
  • Focus only on "how" to teach - emphasize teacher's delivery rather than student learning
  • Ignore the dynamic nature of the learning process

The Shift from Teaching to Learning

Traditional Approach

Lock-step approach that is structured and inflexible:

  • Fixed set of topics for all students
  • Assessment focused on content recall only
  • Everyone learns the same thing in the same way
  • No room for flexibility or student interests
  • Focus on what students know, not how they can use it
Modern Approach

Focus on learner's active participation, personal needs, and meaningful engagement:

  • Learning based on students' individual needs
  • Develops each student's unique abilities
  • Uses variety of teaching techniques, not just one method
  • Stimulates interest and makes learning engaging
  • Encourages active participation from both teachers and learners

Influenced by constructivist and communicative theories

The Evolving Role of the Teacher

Traditional Model: "The sage on the stage" - teacher delivers knowledge while students passively receive it.

Criticism: This model fails to foster deep learning, critical thinking, or meaningful engagement with content.

Traditional Teacher Roles by Method

  • Grammar-Translation Method: Explainer of rules
  • Direct Method: Model and guide
  • Audio-Lingual Method: Provider of drills for repetition/memorization
  • Total Physical Response: Giver of commands
  • The Silent Way: Silent guide
  • Suggestopedia: Supportive facilitator

Modern Teacher Roles

Facilitator/Guide

"The guide on the side" who supports student discovery

Planner

Designs meaningful learning experiences and curricula

Instructor

Provides clear explanations and demonstrations

Assessor

Evaluates student progress and provides feedback

Mentor

Supports student growth and development

The Nature of Effective Teaching

Research Insight

Recent research reveals that the greatest impact on overall school effectiveness is due to classroom-level factors, rather than school-level factors.

Characteristics of Effective Teachers (Muijs and Reynolds, 2005)

Positive Attitude

Maintain optimistic and encouraging classroom environment

Pleasant Climate

Develop positive social/psychological atmosphere

High Expectations

Believe in what pupils can achieve and challenge them

Lesson Clarity

Communicate objectives and instructions clearly

Time Management

Use classroom time efficiently and purposefully

Lesson Structuring

Organize lessons with strong, logical flow

Varied Methods

Employ diverse teaching approaches and techniques

Incorporating Ideas

Use and build upon pupil contributions

Questioning Skills

Use appropriate and varied questioning techniques

Context-Specific Effectiveness

Effective teaching methods are context specific, depending on factors such as:

  • Type of activity in the lesson
  • Subject matter being taught
  • Pupil backgrounds (age, ability, gender, socio-economic status, ethnicity)
  • Pupils' personal characteristics (personality, learning style, motivation, self-esteem)
Critical Thinking Questions

Discussion Points:

  • How does the shift from teaching-focused to learning-centered approaches change classroom dynamics?
  • What challenges might teachers face when transitioning from traditional to modern teaching roles?
  • How can teachers balance structure and flexibility in their teaching approaches?
  • Why are effective teaching methods context-specific rather than universally applicable?
  • How might technology influence the evolution of teaching methods and teacher roles?
