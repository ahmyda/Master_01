Traditional vs Modern Teaching Approaches

Traditional Teaching
  • Teacher-centered approach
  • Focus on knowledge transmission
  • Rote memorization and repetition
  • Standardized curriculum
  • Passive learning
Modern Teaching
  • Student-centered approach
  • Influenced by communicative and constructivist theories
  • Focus on active learning and critical thinking
  • Personalized learning paths
  • Collaborative and interactive

Methods of Language Teaching

A method is a set of specifications of how teaching should be done. Here are the main language teaching methods:

Grammar-Translation Method (GTM)

Focuses on reading and writing through grammar rules and translation from the target language to the native language.

Direct Method

Teaches through speaking and listening only, using no translation — everything is explained in the target language.

Audio-Lingual Method (ALM)

Uses repetition and drills to build correct language habits through imitation.

Communicative Language Teaching (CLT)

Emphasizes real communication and fluency rather than memorizing rules.

Task-Based Language Teaching (TBLT)

Students learn language by completing meaningful tasks like solving problems or planning activities.

The Silent Way

The teacher stays mostly silent and uses visual tools so students discover the language rules themselves.

Suggestopedia

Creates a relaxed, positive atmosphere with music and role-play to make learning easier.

Total Physical Response (TPR)

Students learn by listening and responding physically to commands and actions.

Natural Approach

Language is learned naturally through understanding and communication before speaking.

Community Language Learning (CLL)

The teacher acts as a counselor while learners work together and support each other in speaking.

Limitations of Teaching Methods

Why might relying too much on methods be limiting for teachers or students?

Methods of language teaching proved to be limited and weak because:

  • Teaching is very complex and cannot be reduced to a single method
  • Methods are very rigid and don't adapt to different contexts
  • They may not suit the different contexts of learning
  • Methods are limited because they focus on teacher delivering content rather than student learning
What are the factors besides method that influence success of teaching?
  • The students' needs and backgrounds
  • The learning environment
  • The teacher-student relationship
  • The available materials and resources
  • The cultural and social context

The Shift from Teaching to Learning

New methods are skill-using not skill-getting → Active learning

The shift from the teacher delivering content to focusing on learners' "active engagement, their needs, their experience, etc." This shift is influenced by many theories:

Communicative Competence Theory

First created by Dell Hymes (1972). Encompasses not only grammatical knowledge of the language but also the ability to use the language effectively in "socially appropriate manner".

Communicative competence is composed of 4 core elements:

Grammatical Competence

Knowledge of language structure and rules

Sociolinguistic Competence

Appropriate use of language in social contexts

Strategic Competence

Ability to use communication strategies effectively

Discourse Competence

Ability to produce coherent and cohesive texts

Constructivism

Key theorists: Jean Piaget, Lev Vygotsky

This theory argues that learners individually discover and build their own knowledge. Learners construct their own meaning by negotiating that meaning with others, making connections with and modifying prior conceptions and knowledge, and addressing content in different contexts.

Key Insight

This shift has moved from "lock-step learner" (fixed set of knowledge) to a new approach based on the learners' needs where learning is personalized and meaningful.

The Evolving Role of the Teacher

Critical Perspective

"The task of education is not to pour information into the learners' heads but rather to engage learners' minds with powerful and useful concepts."

Critics say that the teacher's role should no more confirm the dictum "the sage on the stage" but rather evolve into:

Facilitators

Guiding learning processes rather than dictating content

Planners

Designing learning experiences tailored to student needs

Instructions

Providing clear guidance and scaffolding for learning

Assessments

Evaluating student progress and providing feedback

Mentors

Supporting personal and academic development
