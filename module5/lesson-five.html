1. The Role of Teacher Talk

Teacher talk is not merely about issuing directions or filling classroom silence—it functions as a **scaffolded linguistic input** that shapes learning opportunities (Walsh, 2011). Effective teacher talk is purposeful, context-sensitive, and pedagogically strategic. It should:

  • Explain concepts clearly and accurately, using language that is comprehensible yet challenging (i.e., within learners’ Zone of Proximal Development; Vygotsky, 1978)
  • Organize the lesson flow by signaling transitions, setting expectations, and managing tasks
  • Model authentic language use—including pronunciation, grammar, discourse markers, and pragmatic norms—thus providing a rich input resource for learners (Long, 1996)

Crucially, the **quality and function** of teacher talk matter far more than its quantity. Excessive or redundant teacher talk can inhibit learner participation and limit opportunities for language production (Nunan, 1987).

“Some teachers speak to speak—not to teach.” (Walsh, 2011, p. 12)

In language classrooms—where English serves as both the subject of instruction and the medium of interaction—**maximizing meaningful learner talk** is essential. As Ellis (2008) notes, language acquisition thrives on opportunities for learners to *use* language communicatively, not just hear it.

2. Why Learner Communication Matters

Active learner communication is the engine of language development. When learners engage in authentic interaction, they move beyond passive reception to active construction of meaning (Swain, 1985). Specifically, learner talk supports:

Clarifying understanding
Through negotiation of meaning (e.g., clarification requests, confirmation checks), learners resolve ambiguities and refine comprehension (Long, 1996).
Practicing the target language
Interaction pushes learners from receptive skills (listening/reading) toward productive output (speaking/writing), which is critical for fluency and accuracy development (Swain, 1995).
Developing critical thinking
Expressing opinions, justifying views, and responding to peers fosters higher-order cognitive engagement (Mercer & Littleton, 2007).
Enhancing social interaction skills
Classroom discourse mirrors real-world communicative norms—learners practice turn-taking, active listening, and collaborative dialogue (Walsh, 2011).
Managing conversational structure
Effective participation requires awareness of discourse features like topic maintenance, repair strategies, and backchanneling (Seedhouse, 2004).

3. Classroom Discourse: TTT vs. LTT

Classroom discourse encompasses all verbal (and often nonverbal) interaction that occurs during a lesson. A key analytical distinction is between Teacher Talk Time (TTT) and Learner Talk Time (LTT) (Nunan, 1987).

Acronym Meaning Pedagogical Purpose
TTT Teacher Talk Time Instructional modeling, task framing, feedback, and classroom management
LTT Learner Talk Time Opportunities for language use, meaning negotiation, peer collaboration, and fluency practice

The goal is not to minimize TTT at all costs, but to ensure it is **strategic and facilitative**. As Walsh (2011) emphasizes, high-quality TTT that scaffolds learning is more valuable than low-quality LTT that lacks purpose. Ideal lessons create **extended, meaningful LTT episodes**—such as pair discussions, problem-solving tasks, or opinion-sharing—where learners co-construct knowledge.

4. Types of Classroom Discourse (Kramsch, 1998)

Kramsch (1998) conceptualizes classroom discourse along a continuum from pedagogic to natural discourse. This framework helps teachers reflect on the communicative authenticity of classroom interaction.

Pedagogic Discourse Natural Discourse
Formal, structured, teacher-initiated Informal, spontaneous, learner-initiated
Rigid roles: teacher as authority, learners as recipients Fluid roles: co-construction of meaning through collaboration
Focus on linguistic accuracy and curriculum coverage Focus on communicative fluency and authentic expression
Known-answer (display) questions dominate Genuine information gaps drive interaction
Ideal Practice: A Balanced Approach

Teachers might begin with pedagogic discourse to explicitly teach a grammar point (e.g., past perfect), then transition to natural discourse during a storytelling task where learners recount personal experiences using the target structure. This mirrors the “focus on form within a focus on meaning” approach (Long, 1991).

Kramsch (1998, p. 35) argues that while pedagogic discourse is necessary for systematic learning, overreliance on it can create an “artificial communicative environment” that fails to prepare learners for real-world interaction.

5. Classroom Interactional Competence (Walsh, 2011)

Walsh (2011) introduces Classroom Interactional Competence (CIC) as a core professional skill for language teachers. CIC refers to:

“The ability to use interaction as a tool for mediating and assisting learning.” (Walsh, 2011, p. 2)

CIC encompasses awareness of interactional patterns and the ability to adapt talk to support learning. Both teachers and learners can develop CIC through reflective practice and feedback.

Key Teacher Interactional Strategies

Strategy Description & Rationale
Interactional control Managing turn allocation, topic flow, and participation equity ensures all learners have voice (Walsh, 2006). Poor control can lead to dominant speakers monopolizing talk.
Speech modification Adjusting speech rate, lexical complexity, and redundancy enhances comprehensibility without oversimplifying (foreigner talk vs. teacher talk; Ellis, 2008).
Elicitation techniques Using open-ended prompts, wait time, and follow-up questions draws out deeper thinking and extended output (Chaudron, 1988).

Question Types and Their Impact

Display Questions Referential Questions
Teacher knows the answer (e.g., “What’s the past tense of ‘go’?”) Teacher does not know the answer (e.g., “What did you do last weekend?”)
Purpose: assess knowledge, practice form Purpose: exchange real information, encourage fluency
Typical in IRF (Initiation-Response-Feedback) sequences (Sinclair & Coulthard, 1975) Promote extended discourse and genuine communication

While display questions have a place in controlled practice, **referential questions** are more effective for developing communicative competence because they simulate authentic conversation (Brown, 2001). Skilled teachers strategically blend both, but aim to increase referential questioning as learners progress.

Quick Summary

Purposeful classroom discourse fosters language acquisition by balancing structure and authenticity. Effective teaching prioritizes meaningful interaction over mere verbal coverage.

  • Goal: Maximize *meaningful* LTT—not just reduce TTT
  • Balance: Use pedagogic discourse for instruction, natural discourse for practice (Kramsch, 1998)
  • Core skill: Develop Classroom Interactional Competence (CIC) (Walsh, 2011)
  • Best practice: Favor referential questions, provide wait time, encourage peer-to-peer dialogue

Example in practice: Instead of asking “Is this sentence correct?” (display), ask “How would you rephrase this to sound more natural in a job interview?” (referential + contextualized).
