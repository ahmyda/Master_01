What Is Translation?

Several scholars have attempted to define translation. Understanding the nature of translation is fundamental to becoming an effective translator.

Nida and Taber (1969)
Define translation as the reproduction of the closest natural equivalent of the source language message, first in terms of meaning and secondly in terms of style.
"This definition highlights that meaning takes priority over form in successful translation."
- Core Principle of Translation

Translation is the process of transferring meaning from a source language (SL) to a target language (TL) while respecting context, culture, and the communicative purpose of the text.

Key Insight

Translation is not a word-for-word replacement. The translator's main task is to convey meaning accurately and naturally.

Source Language (SL)
The original language of the text being translated.
Target Language (TL)
The language into which the text is being translated.

Translation as Process and Product

Translation can be viewed from two complementary perspectives that highlight different aspects of the translator's work.

Translation as a Process
Involves comprehension of the source text, transfer of meaning, and reformulation in the target language.
Translation as a Product
Refers to the final translated text produced by the translator (Hatim & Munday, 2004).
Understanding the Dual Nature

The process perspective focuses on the cognitive and decision-making activities of the translator, while the product perspective evaluates the outcome of those activities.

Translation vs Interpretation

Translation and interpretation are related forms of cross-linguistic communication that differ mainly in mode and process.

Translation
Involves the written transfer of meaning from a source text to a target text, allowing time for analysis, revision, and the use of reference materials to ensure accuracy and stylistic appropriateness (Newmark, 1988; Munday, 2016).
Interpretation
Refers to the oral rendering of spoken discourse in real time, requiring immediate comprehension and production with no opportunity for revision (PÃ¶chhacker, 2004).
"Translation prioritizes textual precision and coherence, whereas interpretation emphasizes speed and communicative clarity under time constraints."
- Hatim & Munday (2004)

Cognitive Differences

Translation requires deep analytical thinking and attention to detail, while interpretation demands rapid processing, excellent memory, and quick decision-making under pressure.

Who Is a Translator?

The translator plays an active role as a mediator between languages and cultures, making interpretive choices that shape how texts are received in the target culture.

"Translators make interpretive choices that shape how texts are received in the target culture."
- Venuti (2008)

Essential Translator Competencies:

Linguistic Competence
High proficiency in both source and target languages, including grammar, vocabulary, and style.
Cultural Awareness
Understanding of cultural references, norms, and values in both source and target cultures.
Textual Sensitivity
Ability to analyze text types, register, tone, and rhetorical strategies.
Research Skills
Proficiency in using reference materials, terminology databases, and specialized resources.
The Translator as Mediator

A translator is not just a language technician but a cultural mediator who bridges gaps between different linguistic communities and their worldviews.

Literal Translation: Advantages and Limitations

Literal translation focuses on word-for-word correspondence between the source and target languages, but its effectiveness depends on context and purpose.

When Literal Translation Works:

  • Technical texts - Where precision and consistency are paramount
  • Legal documents - Where exact terminology must be maintained
  • Parallel structures - When languages share similar syntactic patterns
  • Cultural transparency - When concepts are directly transferable

Limitations of Literal Translation:

  • Idiomatic expressions - Literal translation of idioms often fails
  • Cultural references - May be meaningless or confusing when translated literally
  • Syntactic differences - Word order and grammatical structures vary between languages
  • Stylistic naturalness - Can produce awkward or unnatural target texts
Meaning-Based Translation
Prioritizes communicative effect and naturalness in the target language over word-for-word correspondence (Newmark, 1988).
"While literal translation may be appropriate in limited contexts, excessive reliance on it can result in unnatural or misleading target texts."
- Translation Principle

Practice Activity

Translate the following expressions into the target language and explain your choice of translation strategy:

1. He kicked the bucket.
Consider: Is this a literal action or an idiomatic expression? How would you convey the meaning naturally in your target language?
2. Break the ice.
Consider: What is the communicative purpose of this expression? How would you maintain the metaphorical meaning while ensuring naturalness?
Translation Strategy Considerations

For each expression, consider whether to use: (1) Literal translation, (2) Cultural adaptation, (3) Explanation, or (4) Equivalent idiom in the target language.

Discussion Questions:

Reflection

Critical reflection is essential for developing translation competence and understanding the deeper principles of the profession.

What makes translation difficult?
Consider factors such as: cultural gaps, linguistic asymmetry, ambiguous meanings, stylistic differences, and the need to balance fidelity with naturalness.
Why is meaning more important than words in translation?
Reflect on how: communication effectiveness depends on conveying intended meaning, words alone may misrepresent the message, and successful translation requires understanding context and purpose.
"The translator's main task is not to translate words but to translate meanings, not forms but functions, not structures but messages."
- Translation Philosophy
Learning Objective

By the end of this lesson, you should understand the fundamental difference between literal translation and meaning-based translation, and recognize when each approach is appropriate.
