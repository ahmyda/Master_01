Interlanguage (IL)

Concept & Historical Context
Interlanguage emerged as a response to theoretical and empirical limitations of Contrastive Analysis (CA) and Error Analysis (EA):
  • Shift from behaviourist to cognitive theories in psychology
  • L1 transfer alone could not explain all learner language phenomena
  • EA focused narrowly on errors at a point in time, not on developmental processes

Interlanguage views learner language as a dynamic, rule-governed system evolving across stages of L2 acquisition.

Key Terminology
The concept has been described using various terms:
  • “Approximative systems” (Nemser, 1971)
  • “Transitional competence” (Corder, 1967)
  • “Interlanguage (IL)” (Selinker, 1969) — now the most widely used term

IL represents the learner’s intermediate linguistic system between L1 and L2.

Cognitive Processes in Interlanguage (Selinker, 1972)

Five Central Processes
  1. L1 Transfer: Influence of the learner’s native language.
  2. Transfer of Training: Effects of instruction or teacher input.
  3. L2 Learning Strategies: Learner’s approaches to acquiring new forms.
  4. L2 Communication Strategies: Methods used to convey meaning during interaction.
  5. Overgeneralization of L2 Rules: Extending target-language rules beyond their correct use (e.g., “goed” for “went”).

These processes reflect both psychological (1, 3, 5) and social (2, 4) dimensions of acquisition.

Interlanguage vs. Natural Languages

Similarities
  • Systematic: Governed by internal rules (e.g., consistent word order).
  • Internally consistent: Functions as a coherent linguistic system.
  • Universal constraints: Obeys principles common to all languages (e.g., presence of singular/plural; deep/surface structure).
Differences
  • Reduced systems: Fewer and simpler grammatical, lexical, and pragmatic rules.
  • Permeability: IL rules are unstable and constantly revised as learning progresses.
  • Fossilization: Certain errors become permanent despite continued exposure or instruction.

Fossilization

Fossilization refers to the permanent fixation of non-target-like forms in a learner’s IL, resistant to further instruction or input.

Selinker & Lamendella (1978) identified key causes:

  • Low motivation (psychological/social factors)
  • Advanced age (e.g., persistent foreign accent)
  • Limited or low-quality L2 input

IL Methodology

Interlanguage analysis integrates insights from both CA and EA by comparing three data sources:

  • L1 utterances produced by the learner
  • IL utterances (the learner’s version of the L2)
  • Native-speaker L2 utterances

This triangulation allows teachers to:

  • Understand the learner’s transitional competence beyond isolated errors
  • Design instruction aligned with developmental stages
  • Infer cognitive and linguistic processes driving acquisition

Criticism of Interlanguage Theory

Key Limitations:

  • No agreed-upon method for describing how IL systems change over time.
  • Requires extensive longitudinal data (spanning years) to validate developmental claims.
  • Observing “true” IL performance is complex—affected by context, task type, social dynamics, and speech mode (monitored vs. spontaneous).

Critical Thinking Questions

  • How does the Interlanguage perspective shift the focus from “error correction” to “developmental progression” in language teaching?
  • Why might fossilization occur even in highly motivated learners with rich input?
  • How can teachers use IL insights to tailor instruction to learners at different proficiency stages?
  • What challenges arise when trying to collect authentic IL data in classroom settings?
