Historical Context

"In the late 1960s and early 1970s, several researches pointed out that the language of second language learners is systematic and that learner errors are not random mistakes but evidence of rule governed behavior."

Applied Linguistics has viewed errors not merely by native speakers, but also by non-native speakers, recognizing the systematic nature of learner language.

1. Contrastive Analysis (CA)

"We can predict and describe the patterns that will cause difficulty in learning, and those that will not cause difficulty, by comparing systematically the language and culture to be learned with the native language and culture of the student."
- Lado, 1957
Pioneering Work
Lado was the first to provide comprehensive theoretical treatment and systematic procedures for contrastive study of languages.
Systematic Comparison
CA involves describing languages (using structuralist linguistics), comparing them, and predicting learning difficulties.
Behaviorist Foundation
Based on behaviorist theories where language learning was viewed as habit formation reinforced by existing habits.

Lee's Four Assumptions (1968)

Key Principles:
  • The prime cause of difficulty in L2 learning is interference from L1
  • Greater differences between languages lead to more acute learning difficulties
  • Language comparison predicts difficulties and errors in L2 learning
  • Teaching content equals the sum of differences established by CA

Theory of Transfer

Type of Transfer Description Effect on Learning
Positive Transfer Similar patterns between L1 and L2 Facilitates learning, easier to grasp
Negative Transfer Dissimilar patterns between L1 and L2 Impedes learning, requires more effort
Real Example - Arab Learners:
  • Omitting third person '-s' (*he come instead of he comes)
  • Overusing Past Simple instead of Present Perfect (*Did you see Ahmed today? instead of Have you seen Ahmed today?)

2. CA Hierarchy of Difficulty

Stockwell et al. (1965) established a hierarchy of difficulty from greatest to least:

Level Description Example (Arabic → English)
Split L1 has one form, L2 has two or more Arabic has /b/, English has /p/ and /b/
New Category Exists in L2 but not in L1 Arabic lacks indefiniteness markers ('a', 'an')
Absent Category Exists in L1 but absent in L2 Arabic has grammatical gender, English has natural gender
Coalesced Forms L1 has multiple forms, L2 has one Arabic has multiple negators, English mainly uses 'not'
Complete Correspondence Same or similar forms in both languages Simple Past Tense available in both languages

3. CA Methodology

Traditional Five Steps

Procedure:
  • Select compatible tasks for comparison
  • Ensure theoretical compatibility (deep structure)
  • Use same descriptive model for both languages
  • Identify similarities and contrasts
  • Develop teaching materials based on predictions

Enhanced Three-Step Approach

Hypothesis Building & Testing
Develop and empirically test predictions using learner performance data
Data Analysis
Analyze data and accept/reject hypotheses based on evidence
Material Development
Create teaching materials based on validated results

Practical Applications

  • Most effective for homogeneous classrooms (e.g., teaching English only to Arab learners)
  • Helps in curriculum design and test item selection
  • Prevents L1 transfer and remedies errors
  • Exploits similarities between languages

4. Criticism of Contrastive Analysis

Major Limitations:
  • Over-reliance on behaviorism: Neglects cognitive aspects of language learning
  • Researcher limitations: Requires comprehensive knowledge of both languages
  • Overprediction of errors: Not all predicted difficulties materialize in practice
  • Underprediction of errors: Fails to account for developmental errors like *goed, *comed
  • Limited explanatory power: Doesn't fully explain the learning process
Example of Underprediction:

Errors like *goed, *comed, *hurted result from overgeneralization of regular patterns, not L1 interference, and are common in both L2 learners and children learning L1.

Quick Summary

Contrastive Analysis was a dominant approach in the 1960s-1970s that systematically compared languages to predict learning difficulties based on L1-L2 differences.

  • Core idea: L1 habits interfere with L2 learning, with similarities facilitating and differences impeding acquisition
  • Key contribution: Hierarchy of difficulty predicting specific learning challenges
  • Main limitation: Overemphasis on behaviorism and inability to explain all error types
  • Modern relevance: Still useful for specific contexts, especially homogeneous classrooms
