Error Analysis (EA)

Historical Context
During the 1950s-60s, Contrastive Analysis (CA) was dominant, but researchers realized not all L2 learning errors could be explained by L1-L2 differences.
  • CA focused on teachers rather than learners
  • Shift from teaching to learning processes
  • Concentration on errors learners regularly produce
Definition & Purpose
EA is a technique that describes and explains the systematic nature of deviations or errors in learner language.
  • Showed CA couldn't predict most errors
  • Reveals learners' faulty inferences about L2 rules
  • Provides feedback to teachers and learners

Errors vs Mistakes

Mistakes

Performance errors that are random guesses or slips in utilizing a known system correctly.

  • Occur in both native and second language
  • Can be self-corrected by speakers
  • Caused by fatigue, excitement, etc.
  • Not reflective of competence deficiency
Errors

Noticeable deviations reflecting the learner's current competence level in the target language.

  • Systematic deviations
  • Cannot be self-corrected
  • Product of current L2 development stage
  • Categorized as: overgeneralization, incomplete rule application, false concepts

EA Methodology

8-Step EA Process (Sridhar 1980)
  1. Collection of errors: From compositions, exams, or designed tests
  2. Identification of errors: Determining deviation from L2 norms
  3. Classification of error types
  4. Statement of relative frequency of error types
  5. Identification of areas of difficulty in L2
  6. Determination of error sources (L1 transfer, overgeneralization, etc.)
  7. Determination of error seriousness in communication
  8. Remedy by teacher through drills, lessons, activities

Classification of Errors

Errors of Competence

Application of L2 rules which do not correspond to L2 norms. These errors take time to correct.

Interlingual (Transfer Errors)

  • Phonological: /b/ for /p/
  • Morphological: 'book Ahmad' for 'Ahmad's book'
  • Syntactic: 'Huda student' for 'Huda is a student'
  • Semantic: 'close the TV' for 'turn off the TV'
Errors of Performance

Rightly called mistakes. Recognized by speakers and corrected easily.

Intralingual (Overgeneralization Errors)

  • Phonological: Vowels in 'count' pronounced as in 'country'
  • Morphological: 'comed' for 'came'
  • Semantic: Literal translation of idioms like 'kick the bucket'
  • Syntactic: 'He is comes' for 'He is coming'

EA vs Contrastive Analysis (CA)

Error Analysis (EA)
  • Starts with actual errors in L2 learning
  • Studies errors in broader framework of sources and significance
  • Provides data on actual attested problems
  • Forms efficient basis for pedagogical strategies
  • Studies both interlingual and intralingual errors
  • Views learner as active participant in hypothesis formation
Contrastive Analysis (CA)
  • Starts with comparison of L1 and L2 systems
  • Predicts areas of difficulty or error
  • Faces complex theoretical problems like equivalence
  • Focuses mainly on the teacher rather than learner
  • Studies primarily interlingual errors (interference)
  • Was dominant in 1950s-60s but found limited in explaining errors

Criticism of Error Analysis

Limitations and Challenges:

  • Errors may be wrongly classified between language tasks. The same error may be classified as both interlingual and intralingual.
  • EA stresses only what learners cannot do at a given point. It doesn't provide insight into the progression of L2 learning.
  • Errors are contrasted with L2 norms that depend on context, formality, and researcher-learner relations.
  • Learners may avoid structures where they'd likely commit errors, skewing EA results.

Critical Thinking Questions

  • How does Error Analysis provide a more learner-centered approach compared to Contrastive Analysis?
  • What are the practical implications of distinguishing between errors and mistakes in language teaching?
  • How might the limitations of Error Analysis affect its application in diverse classroom contexts?
  • What strategies could teachers use to address both interlingual and intralingual errors identified through EA?
