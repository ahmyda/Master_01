1. Defining Applied Linguistics

"Applied Linguistics means many things to many people." (Cook 2006)

Applied Linguistics (AL) is an interdisciplinary field that addresses real-world language problems. Here are some key definitions:

Mediation between theory and practice
"A mediation between theory and practice." (Kaplan and Widdowson 1992)
Synthesis of research
"A synthesis of research from a variety of disciplines, including linguistics." (Hudson 1999)
Investigation of real-world problems
"AL is the theoretical and empirical investigation of real world problems in which language is a central issue." (Brumfit, 1977)
Problem-solving focus
"The focus of AL is on trying to resolve language based problems that people encounter in the real world..." (Spolsky, 2005)
Interdisciplinary field
"Applied Linguistics is an interdisciplinary field of inquiry that addresses a broad range of language-related issues..." (AAAL)

Applied Linguistics vs. Linguistics Applied

There's an important distinction between these two concepts:

Applied Linguistics Linguistics Applied
Recognizes that linguistic insights require interpretation Assumes problems can be solved by direct application of linguistic concepts
Multilateral process involving multiple perspectives Unilateral application of linguistics
Draws on psychology, sociology, education, etc. Primarily relies on linguistics alone
Mediates between theory and practice Applies linguistic theory directly

Classification of Language Problems

Type of Problem Examples
Problems of vocabulary and meaning Understanding idioms, technical terminology
Problems of spelling English spelling irregularities, homophones
Problems of grammar Verb tense usage, article selection
Problems of writing Academic writing conventions, coherence
Problems of developing new scripts Creating writing systems for unwritten languages
Language assessment problems Designing valid language proficiency tests
Language contact problems Code-switching, language interference
Language teaching problems Methodology selection, material development
Language learning problems Motivation issues, learning strategies
Language translation problems Cultural equivalence, idiom translation

2. The Scope of Applied Linguistics

While initially focused on language teaching, Applied Linguistics has expanded to include many areas:

Language and Education

Examples:
  • First-language education: A child studying their home language(s)
  • Additional-language education:
    • Second-language education: Studying the society's majority language
    • Foreign-language education: Studying another country's language
  • Clinical linguistics: Study and treatment of speech and communication impairments

Language Testing

Example:

Designing a proficiency test for university admission that accurately measures a student's ability to use academic English in reading, writing, listening, and speaking.

Language, Work, and Law

Area Description Example
Workplace communication Study of language use in workplace and power relations Analyzing how language creates hierarchy in corporate meetings
Language planning Decisions about official language status and institutional use Establishing bilingual education policies
Forensic linguistics Using linguistic evidence in legal investigations Determining authorship of anonymous threatening letters

Language, Information, and Effect

Area Description Example
Literary stylistics Relationship between linguistic choices and effects in literature Analyzing how metaphor creates meaning in poetry
Critical Discourse Analysis (CDA) Study of persuasive language and manipulation Examining political speeches for hidden ideologies
Translation and interpretation Principles of equivalence between languages Translating cultural concepts that have no direct equivalent
Information design Arrangement and presentation of written language Designing effective warning labels or instructional manuals
Lexicography Planning and compiling dictionaries and reference works Creating a specialized dictionary for medical terminology

3. The Need for Applied Linguistics

Applied Linguistics is crucial because:

  • It addresses practical language problems in real-world contexts
  • It recognizes that linguistic knowledge alone is often insufficient
  • It draws on multiple disciplines for comprehensive problem-solving
  • It extends beyond language teaching to many areas of human life
Real-World Applications:
  • Improving diagnosis of speech pathologies
  • Designing new writing systems for previously unwritten languages
  • Natural language processing for AI applications
  • Enhancing translator and interpreter training
  • Developing valid language examinations
  • Determining literacy levels in populations
  • Creating effective language teaching materials
  • Providing workplace language training
  • Resolving communication differences between cultural groups

Key Questions in Applied Linguistics

Critical Questions:
  • What language skills should children attain beyond basic literacy?
  • Should children speaking a dialect be encouraged to maintain it or learn the standard form?
  • In multilingual communities, which languages should be used in schools?
  • Should deaf children learn sign language or focus on lip reading and speaking?
  • Should everyone learn foreign languages, and if so, which ones?
  • How should we respond to language change - accept or control it?
  • Should we prevent languages from dying out, and if so, how?
  • Is the growth of English as an international lingua franca positive or negative?
  • Is language being used for political oppression and indoctrination?
  • Which languages should be used in law courts and official documents?

Introducing Applied Linguistics — Brief Summary

Applied Linguistics (AL) studies real-world language problems by combining linguistic theory with other fields (education, sociology, psychology, law, etc.). It’s both interdisciplinary and practical — aiming to solve issues like language teaching, testing, translation, clinical diagnosis, and policy. (source: lecture notes) :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}

  • Core idea: mediation between theory and practice — using research to reframe and solve language problems.
  • AL vs. “Linguistics Applied”: AL is multilateral (uses many disciplines); “linguistics applied” tries to apply only linguistic concepts directly.
  • Major areas: language teaching & acquisition, assessment, sociolinguistics, forensic linguistics, translation, corpus & computational work, information design, lexicography, and clinical linguistics.
  • Why it matters: helps make decisions about schooling languages, dialect policy, literacy, sign language, preservation of endangered languages, and more.

Example: designing a valid language test for migrant learners requires linguistics + measurement theory + knowledge of learners’ social context.
