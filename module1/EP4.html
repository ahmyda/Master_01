Exam Preparation: Interlanguage (Lecture 4)

15 Likely Exam Questions & Model Answers

Q1: What is Interlanguage (IL), and how does it differ from earlier views of learner language?

A: Interlanguage is the learner’s dynamic, rule-governed linguistic system that develops between L1 and L2. Unlike Contrastive Analysis (which saw errors as L1 interference) or Error Analysis (which focused only on mistakes), IL treats learner language as a **coherent, evolving system** with its own internal logic at every stage.

Q2: Who coined the term “Interlanguage,” and what were alternative terms used earlier?

A: Larry Selinker (1969) introduced “Interlanguage.” Earlier terms included Nemser’s (1971) “approximative systems” and Corder’s (1967) “transitional competence.” All describe the same concept: the learner’s intermediate linguistic system.

Q3: List Selinker’s (1972) five cognitive processes that shape IL development.

A: (1) L1 transfer, (2) transfer of training (instructional influence), (3) L2 learning strategies, (4) L2 communication strategies, and (5) overgeneralization of L2 rules.

Q4: What is “fossilization,” and why is it significant in SLA?

A: Fossilization is the **permanent fixation** of non-target-like forms in a learner’s IL, resistant to correction or further input. It shows that not all learners reach native-like proficiency, even with motivation and exposure—highlighting limits to ultimate attainment.

Q5: How is IL similar to natural languages?

A: IL is **systematic** (follows consistent rules), **internally consistent** (functions as a unified system), and **constrained by linguistic universals** (e.g., all languages distinguish singular/plural; use deep/surface structures).

Q6: How does IL differ from natural languages?

A: IL is (1) **reduced** (simpler grammar/lexis), (2) **permeable** (rules constantly revised), and (3) prone to **fossilization** (errors become permanent). Natural languages are stable and fully developed.

Q7: Why is IL considered a cognitive approach to SLA?

A: IL theory views learners as **active hypothesis-testers**, constructing mental grammars through cognitive processes (e.g., overgeneralization, strategy use)—not just imitating or responding to habit formation, as behaviorist models (like CA) suggested.

Q8: How does IL methodology improve upon Error Analysis?

A: While EA only examines errors, IL compares **three data sources**: (1) learner’s L1, (2) learner’s IL, and (3) target L2. This reveals the **full developmental trajectory**, not just deviations.

Q9: Give an example of fossilization in English learners’ speech.

A: A highly proficient non-native speaker consistently says “She go to school” (missing third-person -s) despite years of exposure, correction, and advanced fluency—this error has fossilized.

Q10: What causes fossilization according to Selinker & Lamendella (1978)?

A: Key causes include (1) low motivation, (2) advanced age (especially affecting pronunciation), and (3) limited or low-quality L2 input.

Q11: Can L1 transfer and overgeneralization co-occur in the same error? Explain.

A: Yes. For example, an Arabic speaker says “He write book.” The missing past tense may reflect Arabic (no tense marking → L1 transfer), while the missing article (“a book”) may stem from overgeneralizing article omission → intralingual error. Diagnosis requires careful analysis.

Q12: Why is longitudinal data important in IL research?

A: IL is a **developmental process**—to understand how systems change, researchers need data from the same learner over months or years. Cross-sectional (one-time) data cannot capture progression or fossilization.

Q13: What is a major criticism of Interlanguage Theory?

A: There is **no universally agreed method** to describe how IL systems evolve. Also, collecting authentic, longitudinal IL data is time-consuming and affected by situational factors (e.g., task type, stress), making generalizations difficult.

Q14: How can teachers apply IL insights in the classroom?

A: Teachers can (1) interpret errors as signs of **development**, not failure; (2) avoid overcorrecting fossilizable forms; (3) design instruction aligned with learners’ **current IL stage**; and (4) encourage communication strategies that support hypothesis testing.

Q15: Contrast the focus of CA, EA, and IL.

A: CA predicts errors via **L1-L2 comparison**; EA describes **actual errors** post-hoc; IL explains the **learner’s developmental system** as a whole—integrating both and emphasizing cognitive growth over time.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Confusing “fossilization” with temporary errors (fossilized errors are permanent)
  • Saying IL is “just a collection of errors”—it’s a full linguistic system
  • Attributing all IL features to L1 transfer (intralingual processes are equally important)
  • Claiming every learner fossilizes in the same way—fossilization is individual and domain-specific
