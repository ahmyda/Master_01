Applied Linguistics of Language Teaching

Detailed Analysis

1. Applied Linguistics of Language Teaching

Core Requirements
Applied linguistics needs: (1) a coherent theory of language, (2) rigorous descriptive tools, (3) research relevant to language teaching.

What Constitutes Applied Linguistics?

Required Element Description What It Means
Theory of Language "a coherent theory of language, whether this comes from linguistics or from some other discipline" Must have a systematic framework explaining how language works
Descriptive Tools "a set of rigorous descriptive tools to handle language" Systematic methods for analyzing and describing language
Research Base "a body of research relevant to language teaching" Empirical studies connected to language learning and teaching

What is NOT Applied Linguistics?

Type From PDF Example
No Language Element "if there is no language element" Studying classroom furniture arrangement without analyzing language use
No Theory of Language "If the language elements are handled without any theory of language" Teaching grammar rules without understanding linguistic principles behind them
Folk Ideas Only "methods of language description be based solely on folk ideas from the school tradition of grammar" Teaching "don't end sentences with prepositions" without understanding actual usage
Unrelated Research "If the research base is neither directly concerned with language teaching nor related to it in a demonstrable way" Applying pure mathematics theory to language teaching without linguistic connection
"An idea from mathematical theory, computer simulation or first language acquisition needs to show its credentials by proving its link to second language teaching through L2 evidence and argument, not imposing itself by fiat, by analogy, or by sheer computer modelling."
- Key Principle

Chain of Logic Requirement

External Theory Required Connection Valid Application Invalid Application
First Language Acquisition Must show how L1 processes relate to L2 learning Using L1 morpheme order research to inform L2 teaching sequence Assuming L2 learning exactly mirrors L1 acquisition
Computer Simulation Must model actual language processes Simulating vocabulary acquisition based on memory research Random sentence generation without linguistic basis

2. The Non-Native Language Teacher

"Who is worth more as a language teacher, the native speaker or the non-native speaker?"
- Medgyes (1992, 1994)

Historical Teaching Methods Perspective

Teaching Method Time Period View on Native Teachers View on Non-Native Teachers
Direct Method Late 1800s Essential - provide authentic language model Inadequate - cannot provide pure target language environment
Reading Approach 1920s-1930s Not expected to be available Expected - methods designed for non-native teachers
Communicative Language Teaching 1970s onward Ideal for promoting natural communication Acceptable only when native teachers unavailable

Medgyes' Paradigm Shift (1992, 1994)

Before Medgyes After Medgyes
"native teachers were worth more than non-natives" Questioned this assumption with research
"only non-natives with near-native proficiency could entertain any legitimate aspiration to enjoy a certain prestige" Recognized unique advantages of non-native teachers
No research comparing native vs non-native effectiveness Introduced empirical comparison and research

Advantages of Non-Native Teachers

Based on Medgyes (1994:51), Tang (1997:579), Seidlhofer (1999:235-242)

Advantage Detailed Explanation Practical Classroom Application
Model for Imitation Show achievable proficiency level rather than "unattainable" native perfection "I learned English as an adult, and you can too" demonstration
Teach Learning Strategies Share effective strategies from personal successful learning experience Teaching specific vocabulary memorization techniques that worked for them
High Language Awareness Developed explicit knowledge of language rules through conscious learning Detailed explanations of grammar rules with examples
Anticipate Difficulties Predict specific learning challenges based on L1-L2 differences Preparing special exercises for Arabic speakers learning English articles
Empathy for Students Understand student struggles from personal experience Providing encouragement and specific help for difficult areas
Cultural Mediation Bridge between L1 and L2 cultures, explain cultural nuances Explaining why certain English expressions might seem strange in students' culture
Local Context Knowledge Familiar with local educational systems and requirements Preparing students for specific national exams or university requirements

NS-NNS Continuum Concept

Concept Source Meaning Implication
NS-NNS Continuum General Concept All language teachers exist on a spectrum from native to non-native Professional competence matters more than native status
Language Ownership Widdowson (1994) "A language that is truly international is not owned by any group of speakers" Native speakers don't "own" English
Intelligibility Criterion Modiano (1999) Competence based on "capacity to use language forms intelligible for global community" Communicative effectiveness more important than native-like perfection

3. Language Teachers Roles

Overview of Six Teacher Roles

Role Main Function When Most Useful Caution/ Limitation
Controller Directs all classroom activities Accurate reproduction stage; frontal activities Should be relaxed at practice and production stages
Organiser Organizes students, activities, resources Throughout lesson preparation and execution Requires careful planning and preparation
Assessor Evaluates student performance All stages, but correction approach varies Must use appropriate correction strategy for each stage
Prompter Encourages participation, gives hints When students need support but not answers Must not be too aggressive or take over student roles
Participant Joins activities as partner Whole-class activities, demonstrations Should not join pairwork or groupwork (loses monitoring ability)
Resource Provides information and guidance When students need information or help Modern role: organizer and catalyst rather than sole information source

Detailed Analysis of Each Role

Controller Role

Aspect Details Explanation
Definition "Teachers as controllers are in charge of the class and of the activities going on in groups" Teacher-centered approach with high control
Best Use "useful during the accurate reproduction stage of the lesson and in frontal activities" When precision and accuracy are primary goals
Limitation "this control should be relaxed to some degree" at practice and production stages Too much control stifles creativity and communication

Organiser Role - The Six Areas

Area to Organize Specifics Purpose
Environment "desks and chairs arrangement, culture-related posters, maps, flags etc." Create conducive learning atmosphere
Students "according to language proficiency or language abilities" Optimize learning through appropriate grouping
Activities "based on language skills" Target specific language competencies
Time "maximum five minutes for warm-up, followed by 3Ps (10-15 minutes each)" Maintain lesson pacing and balance
Resources "course book, workbook, handouts etc. must be kept in a well-organised way" Ensure material accessibility and efficiency
Yourself "leave their problems outdoors and focus on the work taking place inside" Maintain professional focus and presence

Assessor Role - Error Correction Strategies

Lesson Stage Correction Strategy Rationale Example
Accurate Reproduction "correcting each student error or mistake" Prevent fossilization of incorrect forms Immediate correction during pronunciation drill
Practice Stage Selective correction Focus on target language point while allowing some fluency Correcting only target grammar structure
Production Stage (Immediate Creativity) "gentile correction or delayed correction" Prioritize communication and fluency over accuracy Note errors, give feedback after activity

Prompter Role

Aspect Details Good Practice Poor Practice
Function "encourages students to participate... makes suggestions about how students may proceed" Give hints without giving answers Provide complete solutions
Caution "has to be performed with discretion because if the teacher is too aggressive, s/he will take over the jobs from the students" Wait, then give minimal prompts Jump in immediately with help
Risk "make the students lazy and passive" Encourage student initiative Create dependency on teacher

Participant Role

Aspect Details Appropriate Participation Inappropriate Participation
Guideline "Teachers should not be afraid to participate in certain activities as a partner" Whole-class games, demonstrations Taking over student roles
Limitation "should not get involved in pairwork or group-work because it will prevent him/her from monitoring the students" Observing and monitoring all groups Joining one pair/group exclusively
Reason "prevent him/her from monitoring the students and performing other important roles" Maintain ability to assess and support all students Lose overview of classroom

Resource Role - Evolution

Era Teacher's Role Details Modern Implication
Past Primary Information Source "Teachers used to be the only resource of information" Teacher knowledge limited what could be taught
Present Information Guide "Students have an access to the Internet and other important sources" Students can access information independently
Modern Role Organizer & Catalyst "Teachers are supposed to organise and coordinate the process of acquisition, and to act as a catalyst" Facilitate learning rather than transmit information

The 3Ps Framework (from Organiser Role)

Stage Time Allocation Teacher Role Student Activity Correction Strategy
Presentation 10-15 minutes Controller Receive new information Immediate correction
Practice 10-15 minutes Assessor/Organiser Controlled exercises Selective correction
Production 10-15 minutes Prompter/Resource Free communication Gentle/Delayed correction
