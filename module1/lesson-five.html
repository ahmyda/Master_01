Introduction

Language learning strategies (LLS) refer to the conscious steps, thoughts, and techniques that learners use to improve the comprehension, acquisition, and retention of a second language. These strategies vary from learner to learner, depending on preferences, goals, needs, and learning contexts.

Researchers such as O’Malley, Chamot, and Oxford contributed significantly to our understanding of how learners manage their learning process. LLS play a central role in developing autonomy, motivation, and effective learning habits.

O’Malley et al. (1985) – Three Main Types of Strategies

Metacognitive Strategies
These strategies involve planning, monitoring, and evaluating learning. Learners using metacognitive strategies think about their own thinking.
  • Planning before starting a task
  • Setting learning goals
  • Monitoring comprehension during reading or listening
  • Evaluating performance after completing a task

Example: A student decides to focus only on verb forms during a grammar lesson to improve accuracy.

Cognitive Strategies
These involve direct manipulation of learning materials.
  • Repetition
  • Summarizing
  • Note-taking
  • Translation
  • Guessing meaning from context

Example: Repeating new vocabulary aloud several times to memorize pronunciation.

Social Strategies
These involve learning through interaction with others.
  • Asking questions for clarification
  • Cooperating with peers
  • Practicing with native speakers

Example: Asking a teacher to give another explanation of a difficult phrase.

Oxford (1990) – Six Categories of Strategies

Oxford proposed a widely used and detailed classification of LLS, dividing them into Direct and Indirect strategies.

Direct Strategies

  • Memory Strategies: Grouping, imagery, creating associations.
  • Cognitive Strategies: Practicing, analyzing, summarizing, translating.
  • Compensation Strategies: Guessing unknown words, using gestures, paraphrasing.

Indirect Strategies

  • Metacognitive Strategies: Planning, monitoring, evaluating.
  • Affective Strategies: Managing emotions, self-encouragement, relaxation.
  • Social Strategies: Cooperation, asking questions, cultural understanding.

Extended Examples for Better Understanding

  • Repetition (Cognitive): Saying “responsibility” 10 times to master stress.
  • Selective Attention (Metacognitive): Listening only for adjectives in a podcast.
  • Using Gestures (Compensation): Acting out a word when you forget the name.
  • Self-Evaluation (Metacognitive): Asking: “Did I meet today’s learning goal?”
  • Lowering Anxiety (Affective): Taking deep breaths before giving a presentation.
  • Cooperation (Social): Working in pairs to check homework answers.

Why Are Learning Strategies Important?

LLS make learners more active, independent, and responsible for their own progress. They help improve memory, comprehension, communication, and overall performance.

  • Enhance motivation
  • Support self-regulated learning
  • Improve problem-solving skills
  • Help overcome learning difficulties

Pedagogical Implications

Teachers should guide students to discover and apply strategies that fit their learning style. Strategy training can be integrated into classroom activities.

  • Teach students how to plan effectively
  • Encourage reflection and self-evaluation
  • Promote cooperation and group work
  • Model the use of different strategies
  • Help learners manage anxiety
