Exam Preparation: Error Analysis (Lecture 3)

15 Likely Exam Questions & Model Answers

Q1: What is Error Analysis (EA), and why did it emerge as a response to Contrastive Analysis?

A: EA is a method that describes and explains systematic deviations in learner language. It emerged because CA failed to predict many real errors (e.g., *goed*, *comed*), which stem from L2 internal overgeneralization—not L1 interference. EA shifted focus from prediction (CA) to observation of actual learner output.

Q2: Distinguish between “errors” and “mistakes” in SLA.

A: Mistakes are performance slips (e.g., saying “he go” when tired)—random, self-correctable, and not reflective of competence. Errors are systematic deviations (e.g., always omitting third-person -s)—reflecting incomplete L2 knowledge and cannot be self-corrected.

Q3: Why is EA considered more learner-centered than CA?

A: CA focused on comparing L1/L2 to help teachers predict difficulties. EA studies the learner’s actual output, treating them as an active hypothesis-former—making it centered on the learner’s cognitive process, not just the teacher’s assumptions.

Q4: List and explain two types of intralingual errors with examples.

A: (1) Overgeneralization: applying a rule too broadly (e.g., *comed* for “came”). (2) False concept formation: misunderstanding L2 vocabulary (e.g., using “big news” to mean “important gossip” based on L1 logic).

Q5: Give two examples of interlingual (transfer) errors by Arab learners of English.

A: (1) Morphological: saying “book Ahmad” instead of “Ahmad’s book” (Arabic possessive structure). (2) Semantic: “close the TV” instead of “turn off the TV” (direct translation from Arabic).

Q6: Outline Sridhar’s (1980) 8-step EA methodology.

A: (1) Collect errors → (2) Identify deviations → (3) Classify error types → (4) Calculate frequencies → (5) Pinpoint L2 difficulty areas → (6) Determine sources (L1 transfer, overgeneralization, etc.) → (7) Assess seriousness → (8) Design remedies (drills, lessons, feedback).

Q7: What is the main difference between interlingual and intralingual errors?

A: Interlingual errors result from **L1 interference** (e.g., Arabic word order in English). Intralingual errors arise from **misuse of L2 rules** (e.g., overgeneralizing regular past tense to irregular verbs like *goed*).

Q8: Why might EA miss some learner difficulties?

A: Learners often **avoid** structures they find difficult (e.g., never using passive voice), so no errors appear in their output—leading to underestimation of challenges (known as “avoidance strategy”).

Q9: How does EA inform teaching practice?

A: By identifying frequent, serious errors and their sources, EA helps teachers: (1) prioritize which errors to address, (2) design targeted corrective feedback, and (3) create materials that clarify misunderstood L2 rules.

Q10: What is a limitation of using native-speaker norms as the standard in EA?

A: L2 “correctness” depends on context, register, and communicative purpose. A sentence may be “non-standard” but fully intelligible (e.g., “He don’t like it”). Rigid adherence to native norms ignores functional communication and linguistic variation.

Q11: Can an error be both interlingual and intralingual? Explain.

A: Yes—classification can be ambiguous. For example, an Arab learner says “She go school.” The missing -s may stem from Arabic (no verb conjugation → interlingual) OR from overgeneralizing “I go, you go” → intralingual. Context and learner background are needed for accurate diagnosis.

Q12: How does EA view the language learner?

A: As an **active constructor of knowledge**—not a passive habit-former (as in CA). Learners form hypotheses about L2 rules, test them through output, and refine understanding based on feedback—a cognitive, creative process.

Q13: Why can’t EA fully explain L2 development?

A: EA only shows what learners **can’t do** at a given time—it doesn’t track **progress over time** or what learners **can do correctly**. It lacks developmental insight compared to approaches like Interlanguage Theory.

Q14: Give one phonological and one syntactic intralingual error.

A: Phonological: pronouncing the /ʌ/ in “count” like /ʌ/ in “country” due to overgeneralized vowel patterns. Syntactic: saying “He is comes” by incorrectly combining present continuous with base verb form.

Q15: Contrast the goals of CA and EA.

A: CA aims to **predict** L2 difficulties by comparing L1/L2 systems before teaching begins. EA aims to **describe and explain** actual errors after they occur, using learner data to understand acquisition processes and improve instruction.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Using “error” and “mistake” interchangeably (they have technical meanings in EA)
  • Assuming all errors come from L1 (ignoring intralingual/developmental sources)
  • Saying EA replaced CA entirely—it complemented it, and CA is still useful in specific contexts
  • Thinking EA tells us how learning happens—it only shows outcomes, not cognitive processes
