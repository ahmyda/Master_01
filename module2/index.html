1. Defining Sociolinguistics

Sociolinguistics is a sub-branch of linguistics that scientifically studies the relationship between language and society. However, this simple definition hides considerable complexity due to:

  • The intricate nature of language structure itself
  • The additional complexity introduced by social aspects
  • The need for alternative tools to explore language use in social contexts

Founding Figures of Sociolinguistics

William Labov

Focused on how social determinants are embedded in language use; developed variationist sociolinguistics

Basil Bernstein

Known for his theory of language codes (restricted vs. elaborated codes)

Dell Hymes

Developed the concept of communicative competence and emphasized knowledge of social norms in communication

John Gumperz

Emphasized interactional sociolinguistics and conversational inference

Charles Ferguson

Introduced the concept of diglossia (situations where two varieties of the same language exist side by side)

Joshua Fishman

Focused on multilingualism, language planning, and language maintenance

"The central dogma of sociolinguistics is that the community is prior to the individual."
Labov (2010, p. 7)
"Speech is not a personal possession, but a social; it belongs, not to the individual, but to the member of society. No item of existing language is the work of an individual; for what we may severally choose to say is not language until it be accepted and employed by our fellows."
Whitney (1867, p. 404)

2. Sociolinguistics vs. Sociology of Language

Since language is both an individual and social possession, it can be studied from two perspectives:

Aspect Micro-sociolinguistics (Sociolinguistics) Macro-sociolinguistics (Sociology of Language)
Focus Individuals and how language functions in communication Large groups and understanding society through language
Level of Analysis Micro-level: individual speakers, conversations Macro-level: societal patterns, language policies
Key Questions How do social factors (age, gender, class) affect language use? Why do languages change? Why does one language dominate others?
Key Concepts Language variation, style-shifting, politeness strategies Speech community, language planning, diglossia
Example Studying how women vs. men use tag questions in conversation Analyzing why English became a global lingua franca

Micro-sociolinguistics

Focus Areas:
  • Examining how social attributes (sex, gender, age, ethnicity, religion, class) are reflected in language use
  • Accounting for variation in individuals' ways of speaking
  • Viewing variation as an important aspect of how language functions

This perspective challenges Ferdinand de Saussure and Noam Chomsky's views that downplay language variation in performance.

Macro-sociolinguistics

Focus Areas:
  • Interaction between large groups of people and language
  • Concepts like speech community and community of practice
  • Questions about language change, language dominance, and language death

3. Key Concepts and Examples

Language Variation Based on Social Factors

Social Factor How It Influences Language Example
Age Different generations use language differently; new slang emerges Teenagers using terms like "lit" or "salty" that older generations may not use
Gender Men and women may use different speech patterns Women sometimes use more tag questions ("isn't it?", "don't you think?")
Social Class Different classes may use different vocabulary, pronunciation In England, pronunciation of "h" in words like "house" varies by class
Ethnicity Ethnic groups may develop distinct language varieties African American Vernacular English (AAVE) has distinct grammatical patterns
Region Geographical location affects accent and vocabulary In the US, "soda" vs. "pop" vs. "coke" for carbonated beverages

Speech Community

Definition:

A group of people who share not only a language variety but also norms for its appropriate use.

Example:

Members of a particular profession (like doctors or lawyers) develop specialized vocabulary and communication styles that mark them as part of that community.

Why Variation Matters

Sociolinguists argue that variation is not a problem to be eliminated but rather:

  • An essential aspect of how language functions
  • A reflection of social identity and group membership
  • A mechanism for language change over time
  • A resource for expressing different social meanings
