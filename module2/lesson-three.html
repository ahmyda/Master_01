1. Language Variation: Why Does Language Differ?

Language is not fixed. It changes depending on who is speaking, where they are, and what the situation is.

We do not all speak the same way because:

  • We come from different regions.
  • We belong to different social groups.
  • We have different levels of education.
  • We speak differently based on context (classroom vs. home).
Example: At home you say: "Give me the salt."
In class during a presentation you say: "Could you please pass me the salt?"
This change happens unconsciously. This is sociolinguistics in action.

2. Idiolect: Your Personal Way of Speaking

Your idiolect is your personal speech fingerprint — no one speaks exactly like you.

It includes:

  • How quickly you speak
  • Which words you prefer
  • Your tone
  • Your favorite expressions
Example:
One friend always says "bro". Another always says "wallah". Another says "seriously?" all the time.
These repeated habits form idiolect.

3. Variety: The Neutral Word

Variety is any systematic and recognizable form of language used by a group.

It is a neutral term — it avoids political arguments like "which is the real language?"

Types of Varieties

  • Regional Variety (language differs by location)
  • Social Variety (differs by class, age, religion, gender)
  • Contextual Variety (Register)
  • Professional Variety (Jargon)
Example:
A doctor says "acute inflammation" instead of "pain" → medical variety.
A gamer says "buff, nerf, lag" → gaming variety.

4. Accent: Pronunciation Only

An accent is how words are pronounced. It does not include grammar or vocabulary.

Example:
British English: "water" → /ˈwɔːtə/
American English: "water" → /ˈwɑːtər/
Algerian Arabic has different accents in Djelfa vs. Algiers.

5. Dialect: Accent + Vocabulary + Grammar

A dialect is a complete form of language. It includes:

  • How words are pronounced (accent)
  • The words chosen (vocabulary)
  • How sentences are formed (grammar)
Example (Algerian Arabic):
Oran: "wesh rak?"
Constantine: "chna7walek?"
Djelfa: "kifach rak?"
Same meaning → different dialects.
"A language is a dialect with an army and a navy."

This means political power decides which speech becomes the "official" language.

6. Register: Language Changes by Situation

Register refers to how we change the way we speak depending on situation, audience, and purpose.

Examples of Registers

  • Formal: "Good morning, ladies and gentlemen."
  • Informal: "Hey guys!"
  • Religious: "Peace be upon him."
  • Scientific: "The data indicates a significant correlation."
Example:
Talking to your professor: "I would like to ask a question."
Talking to your friend: "Bro, wait I wanna ask you something."
Same meaning → different register.
