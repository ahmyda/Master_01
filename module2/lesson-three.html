1. Language Variation and Idiolect

It is a truism that language varies. In sociolinguistics, it is established that everyone has distinguishable speech habits known as an idiolect or “one’s personal dialect” (Crystal, 2008, p. 236).

However, to achieve systematic study, sociolinguists focus not on individual idiolects, but on salient linguistic patterns shared by social or regional groups.

Key Terminology

To describe these patterns, linguists use terms such as:

  • Variety – an umbrella, neutral term
  • Accent – pronunciation features only
  • Dialect – includes grammar, vocabulary, and pronunciation
  • Register – language tied to context or purpose
  • Language – often a political designation

2. Understanding Core Concepts

Variety

The term variety is a neutral cover term for any systematically different form of language (Baker, 2010). It avoids the social or political baggage of “dialect” or “language.”

Crystal (2008) notes variety can refer to:

  • Regional forms (e.g., London English)
  • Socially defined speech (e.g., religious English)
  • Context-specific usage (as a synonym for register, medium, or field)
  • Occupational jargon (e.g., medical or legal language)

Accent

An accent refers exclusively to pronunciation features that signal a speaker’s regional or social origin (Crystal, 2008, p. 30).

Example:

A speaker from Glasgow vs. one from Dublin may use the same words and grammar but differ in how they pronounce them.

Register

A register is a variety of language used in a specific social context or for a particular communicative purpose.

Examples:
  • Scientific register: “photosynthesis,” “hypothesis”
  • Religious register: “thou,” “amen,” “blessed are the meek”
  • Informal register: “gonna,” “wanna,” “cool”

3. Dialect vs. Language: A Political Divide

What Is a Dialect?

Crystal (2008, p. 142) defines a dialect as a “regionally or socially distinctive variety of language.”

Wardhaugh (2006, p. 28) observes that to many people, a dialect is seen as:

“a local non-prestigious (therefore powerless) variety of a real language.”

Large languages like Arabic—spoken across 22 countries—naturally develop numerous dialects, yet speakers often maintain mutual intelligibility.

The Problem with Labels

Not all “dialects” are mutually intelligible:

  • Mandarin and Cantonese are called “dialects of Chinese” but are not mutually intelligible in speech.

Conversely, some “languages” are highly intelligible:

  • Norwegian, Swedish, and Danish are considered separate languages, yet speakers understand one another easily (Chambers & Trudgill, 2002).
“A language is a dialect with an army and navy.”
(Popular sociolinguistic aphorism)

This highlights that the distinction between “language” and “dialect” is often political, not linguistic.
