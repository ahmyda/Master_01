Exam Preparation: Sociolinguistics

15 Likely Exam Questions & Model Answers

Q1: Why is the scientific status of sociolinguistics often questioned?

A: Because some academics believe only natural sciences qualify as “true science,” leading them to dismiss social sciences like sociolinguistics as unscientific.

Q2: According to May, Williams, and Wiggins (2002), how is science defined?

A: Science is “the production of knowledge representing the ‘truth’ about phenomena.”

Q3: What distinguishes scientific knowledge from everyday knowledge, according to Chalmers (1982)?

A: Scientific knowledge is objectively proven, making it reliable, whereas everyday knowledge lacks this objectivity.

Q4: How do sociolinguists strive for objectivity in their research?

A: By minimizing personal biases and using systematic methods to analyze language use in social contexts.

Q5: Name three cultural factors that can challenge objectivity in sociolinguistic research.

A: Religion and belief systems, regional identities and dialects, and societal concepts of femininity and masculinity.

Q6: Why is verifiability important in scientific research, including sociolinguistics?

A: Because scientific knowledge must be based on empirical observation and allow other researchers to replicate and confirm results.

Q7: How did William Labov demonstrate the scientific rigor of sociolinguistics?

A: By using quantitative methods and statistical analysis to correlate linguistic forms with social variables, establishing empirical patterns.

Q8: What is the “pseudoscience” argument against sociolinguistics?

A: Some critics claim sociolinguistics should be labeled pseudoscience—even when it uses scientific methods—due to its subject matter or findings.

Q9: What does Neuman (2014) mean by “junk science” in the context of sociolinguistics?

A: It refers to the dismissal of valid scientific research simply because its findings are opposed by certain advocacy groups or interest parties.

Q10: According to Laugksch (2000), what is scientific literacy?

A: The ability to understand, apply, and engage with scientific knowledge while upholding core scientific values like objectivity and evidence.

Q11: What is quantitative literacy, and why is it relevant to sociolinguistics?

A: Quantitative literacy (or numeracy) is the ability to interpret data presented in graphs, tables, and diagrams—essential for analyzing sociolinguistic variation statistically.

Q12: What key criterion determines whether a discipline like sociolinguistics is scientific?

A: Its adherence to objectivity and empirical verification, not whether it studies natural or social phenomena.

Q13: How does the variationist approach support sociolinguistics’ claim to scientific status?

A: It uses systematic data collection, quantitative analysis, and testable hypotheses to identify patterns in language variation and change.

Q14: What alternative framework does Laugksch (2000) propose for evaluating sociolinguistics?

A: The framework of scientific literacy, which emphasizes understanding and applying scientific principles rather than rigid field boundaries.

Q15: What is the critical insight about evaluating scientific knowledge across disciplines?

A: Scientific validity should be judged by the quality of methods and knowledge produced—not by whether the field studies atoms or accents.
