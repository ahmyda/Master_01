1. Defining Sociolinguistics

Sociolinguistics is a sub-branch of linguistics studying the relationship between language and society. Complexity arises due to:

  • The intricate nature of language structure
  • Social aspects influencing language
  • The need for tools to explore language use in context

Founding Figures

William Labov

Studied how social determinants are embedded in language; developed variationist sociolinguistics.

Basil Bernstein

Known for his theory of language codes (restricted vs. elaborated).

Dell Hymes

Developed communicative competence; emphasized social norms in communication.

John Gumperz

Focused on interactional sociolinguistics and conversational inference.

Charles Ferguson

Introduced diglossia: coexistence of two language varieties.

Joshua Fishman

Studied multilingualism, language planning, and language maintenance.

"The central dogma of sociolinguistics is that the community is prior to the individual."
Labov (2010, p. 7)
"Speech is not a personal possession, but a social; it belongs to the member of society."
Whitney (1867, p. 404)

2. Sociolinguistics vs. Sociology of Language

Language can be studied from two perspectives:

Aspect Micro-sociolinguistics Macro-sociolinguistics
Focus Individuals and communication Large groups and societal patterns
Level Micro-level: conversations, individual speakers Macro-level: societal patterns, language policies
Key Questions How do social factors affect language use? Why do languages change or dominate?
Key Concepts Language variation, style-shifting, politeness Speech community, language planning, diglossia
Example Tag questions by men vs. women English as a global lingua franca

Micro-sociolinguistics

Focus Areas:
  • Social attributes (sex, age, ethnicity, class) reflected in language
  • Variation in individual speech
  • Variation as essential to language function

Macro-sociolinguistics

Focus Areas:
  • Interaction of large groups and language
  • Speech community and community of practice
  • Language change, dominance, and death

3. Key Concepts and Examples

Language Variation

Social Factor Influence on Language Example
Age Generational differences; new slang emerges Teenagers: "lit", "salty"
Gender Different speech patterns Women: more tag questions
Social Class Different vocabulary/pronunciation England: "house" pronunciation varies
Ethnicity Distinct language varieties African American Vernacular English (AAVE)
Region Accent and vocabulary differences US: "soda" vs "pop" vs "coke"

Speech Community

Definition:

A group sharing a language variety and norms for its use.

Example:

Specialized vocabulary in professions (doctors, lawyers) marks membership.

Why Variation Matters

Variation is:

  • An essential part of language function
  • A reflection of social identity
  • A mechanism for language change
  • A tool for expressing social meanings
