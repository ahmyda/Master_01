Core Focus

This lecture explores how social factors influence language use and perception, and how language in turn shapes social identities and relationships.

Language Attitudes

Language attitudes refer to socially shaped evaluative orientations toward languages, varieties, accents, or their speakers. These attitudes are not merely personal preferences but are deeply embedded in social structures.

Components of Language Attitudes:

Component Description Examples
Cognitive Components Beliefs and stereotypes about languages or speakers Thinking certain accents sound "uneducated" or "intelligent"
Affective Components Emotional responses and feelings toward languages Feeling pride, shame, irritation, or warmth toward particular accents
Behavioral Components Actions and reactions based on language attitudes Discrimination, accommodation, ridicule, or preferential treatment
"Language attitudes are central to the reproduction of social hierarchies, group boundaries, and linguistic inequality."
- Sociolinguistic Principle
Social Function

Language attitudes serve social functions: they maintain social hierarchies, mark group boundaries, and reproduce systems of privilege and disadvantage based on linguistic features.

Accents and Social Perception

Accents carry powerful social meanings that influence how speakers are perceived and evaluated in various contexts.

Reflection Questions on Accent Perception:

Which English accent do you associate with intelligence or education?
Consider Received Pronunciation (RP), General American, or other standard varieties.
Which accent sounds friendly or trustworthy to you?
Often regional accents or familiar local varieties.
Which accent do you expect to sound less serious or less competent?
Often stigmatized regional or social accents.
Where do you think these judgments come from?
School, media, personal experience, popular culture.
Critical Awareness

Our accent evaluations are not based on intrinsic linguistic qualities but on social associations we've learned through various institutions and experiences.

Further Reflection Questions:

Which accent feels "neutral" or "standard" to you? Why?
The concept of a "neutral" accent is itself socially constructed.
At which accent did you first smile or laugh?
Some accents are culturally marked as humorous or unserious.
Did any accent trigger a stereotype in your mind?
Accents often activate social stereotypes about intelligence, education, or personality.

The Same Speaker Experiment

A revealing sociolinguistic demonstration involves the same speaker using different accents and observing how perceptions change.

What Changed in Perception
The voice (accent) and perceived identity of the speaker.
What Changed in Evaluation
Listeners' judgments about the speaker's intelligence, trustworthiness, education, etc.
"Same speaker. Different accent. Different judgment."
- Sociolinguistic Demonstration

Critical Questions:

  • Which accent would you prefer them to use? Why?
  • What does this reveal about your own language attitudes?
  • How do accent perceptions affect social opportunities?
Experimental Insight

When the same individual speaks with different accents, listeners attribute different personality traits, intelligence levels, and social backgrounds to them, demonstrating that accent perceptions are socially constructed rather than based on individual merit.

Real-World Language Attitudes

Language attitudes manifest in everyday social interactions and institutional practices around the world.

Historical Example (1950s Scandinavia)
"Danish is not a language, but a throat disease," wrote one Norwegian respondent in a postal questionnaire about Scandinavian languages' aesthetic qualities.
Example 1: Regional Accent Hierarchy
"So I think across the island the stronger your accent, the more working class it's considered."
Example 2: Québec Language Dynamics
"Here in Québec, it depends on the language. If you're an anglophone speaking French with an accent then it's likely you'll receive ridicule. If you're a francophone speaking English with an accent then people don't even bat an eye at it."

What Do People Evaluate?

When people express preferences for certain languages or accents, they are rarely evaluating linguistic features alone. Instead, they are responding to:

  • Social status of the speakers
  • Political power associated with the language
  • Cultural stereotypes about the speech community
  • Historical relationships between language groups
  • Media representations of particular accents
Social Meanings

Language attitudes carry complex social meanings about class, ethnicity, education, regional identity, and national belonging that reflect and reproduce social hierarchies.

Case Study: Accent Modification

The 2007 controversy involving Scottish actor Sean Connery and British Prime Minister Gordon Brown reveals contrasting cultural attitudes toward accent modification.

The Controversy
Sean Connery accused Gordon Brown of deliberately softening his Scottish accent to appeal to English voters, suggesting Brown was attempting to "ride two horses" politically.
Journalistic Interpretation
The journalist suggested Brown was seeking to "disguise his native tongue," raising questions about authenticity, betrayal, and the relationship between accent, power, and legitimacy.

Anglo-British Context:

  • Accent is tightly linked to class and region
  • Associated with perceptions of trustworthiness and authenticity
  • Accent modification may be perceived as inauthentic or opportunistic
  • Viewed as betraying one's origins or social class

French Tradition:

  • Accent reduction is often associated with education and professionalism
  • Linked to upward mobility and social advancement
  • Generally praised rather than condemned
  • Viewed as mastering the standard language of power and education
"The social meaning of accent modification varies cross-culturally, reflecting different ideologies about language, identity, and social mobility."
- Cross-cultural Sociolinguistics

Research Reflection

A fundamental question in sociolinguistics concerns the origins of language attitudes and evaluations.

Critical Research Question
How could you test whether people's opinions about a language are based on the intrinsic linguistic features of the language (such as its sounds and grammatical patterns) or derive from non-linguistic factors such as the social and political status of the speakers?

Possible Research Approaches:

Method Approach What It Tests
Matched Guise Technique Same speaker using different accents/varieties Whether evaluations change when linguistic features change but speaker doesn't
Verbal Guise Studies Different speakers using same linguistic content Whether evaluations differ based on speaker characteristics alone
Content Analysis Analyzing media representations of accents How institutions shape language attitudes
Longitudinal Studies Tracking attitude changes over time How social/political changes affect language perceptions
Experimental Design Challenge

Designing experiments to separate linguistic from social factors is challenging because in real life, linguistic features are never encountered in isolation from their social contexts and associations.

For Further Reflection:

  • What methodologies could isolate linguistic from social factors?
  • How do media representations shape our accent perceptions?
  • Can language attitudes be changed through education?
  • What are the ethical implications of accent modification?

Key Takeaways

This lecture has introduced fundamental concepts about how language attitudes function in society and shape social relationships.

Language Attitudes Are Social
They are not personal preferences but socially shaped orientations that reflect and reproduce social hierarchies.
Accents Carry Social Meanings
Accents are interpreted through social filters that associate them with intelligence, trustworthiness, education, class, and region.
Perception ≠ Reality
Our evaluations of accents and languages tell us more about social stereotypes than about linguistic quality or speaker capability.
Cross-Cultural Variation
Attitudes toward accent modification and standardization vary across cultural contexts, reflecting different language ideologies.
Researchable Phenomena
Language attitudes can be studied systematically using sociolinguistic research methods to understand their origins and impacts.
"Understanding language attitudes is essential for addressing linguistic discrimination, promoting language rights, and creating more inclusive educational and professional environments."
